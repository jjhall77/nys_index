## Key New Insights

- **Von Hirsch & Jareborg (1991)** provides the philosophical foundation that Greenfield & Paoli later built on, but its ordinal/non-operational nature is precisely why the CHI movement pivoted to sentencing-based cardinal weights
- **Sherman (2007) and (2013)** are far more foundational than previously reflected — they contain the conceptual architecture (power few, fungibility fallacy, Triple-T) that the CCHI formalized
- **Ignatans & Pease (2016)** opens a genuinely new debate position: victim-assessed seriousness as an alternative to legislative sentencing weights. This challenges Sherman's "democratic legitimacy" argument but faces psychometric problems (non-additivity, framing effects)
- **Fenimore (2019)** provides the first US evidence for harmspot ≠ hotspot divergence, strengthening the case for block-level CHI in NYC
- **Wallace et al. (2009)** — the Canadian CSI has been running since 2009, making it the longest-running national harm index. Its use of actual sentencing data makes it vulnerable to the Lewis et al. (2024) critique
