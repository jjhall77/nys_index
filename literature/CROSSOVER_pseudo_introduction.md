### Opening

Every police department in the United States publishes crime statistics that count a murder and a shoplifting as the same thing: one crime. This arithmetic — treating qualitatively different harms as interchangeable units — is not a neutral act of bookkeeping. It is an implicit assertion that all crimes are equally serious, embedded in the institutional architecture of American policing since the Uniform Crime Reports began in 1930 (Sellin, 1931). The Crime Harm Index movement, launched by Sherman, Neyroud, and Neyroud (2016), proposes to replace this equal-weight convention with one that weights each crime by the severity of its recommended sentence, producing a single aggregate number — total harm — expressed in days of imprisonment.

The CHI has been adopted or adapted in over a dozen countries (van Ruitenburg & Ruiter, 2023). It has demonstrated that crime counts and crime harm can move in opposite directions (Sherman et al., 2016; Andersen & Mueller-Johnson, 2018), that harm concentrates far more intensely than volume at micro-geographic scales (Weinborn et al., 2017), and that harm-weighted targeting identifies places, offenders, and victims that count-based methods miss entirely (Dudfield et al., 2017; Ratcliffe & Kikuchi, 2019). But the CHI literature has developed almost entirely within criminology, engaging only with the crime seriousness tradition (Sellin & Wolfgang, 1964; Rossi et al., 1974) and the evidence-based policing framework (Sherman, 2007, 2013).

This insularity has left the CHI disconnected from three bodies of scholarship that bear directly on its validity, its limitations, and its consequences:

1. **The composite-indicator methodology literature** (Nardo et al., 2008; Saltelli, 2007; Ravallion, 2012), which provides the canonical framework for constructing, testing, and validating any weighted index — including detailed protocols for sensitivity analysis, robustness testing, and transparency about embedded trade-offs.

2. **Social choice theory** (Arrow, 1951; Sen, 1999), which establishes the formal conditions under which heterogeneous values can — and cannot — be aggregated into a single ordering, and which provides the theoretical justification for the "partial comparability" that any sentencing-based CHI implicitly claims.

3. **The sociology of commensuration** (Espeland & Stevens, 1998; Porter, 1995; Bevan & Hood, 2006; Jerven, 2013), which examines what happens when qualitatively different things are transformed into a common metric — including constitutive effects on institutions, gaming behavior, and the political authority conferred by quantification.

This paper brings these three traditions into conversation with the CHI for the first time. We construct a Crime Harm Index for New York City using midpoints of New York State Penal Law sentencing ranges, aggregated to the physical block level for place-based intervention targeting. We then subject this index to the full battery of tests that the composite-indicator literature prescribes — tests that no prior CHI has performed — and frame the results within the theoretical vocabularies of social choice and commensuration.

### The Problem of Commensuration

A Crime Harm Index is, at its core, an act of commensuration. It transforms murder, rape, robbery, assault, burglary, larceny, and sex crimes into a single metric — days of recommended imprisonment — and sums them. This operation is not merely technical. Espeland and Stevens (1998) define commensuration as "the transformation of different qualities into a common metric," and identify it as one of the most consequential forms of social action: it reduces complex realities to numbers, discards contextual information, creates standardized proxies for elusive qualities, and reconstructs the authority relationships of the institutions that adopt it.

The CHI literature has not engaged with this characterization. Sherman et al. (2016) justify sentencing-based weights on three grounds — democratic (derived from elected government's sentencing policy), reliable (consistently applied), and affordable (no new data collection) — without addressing the deeper question of what is gained and lost when qualitatively different harms are forced onto a single scale. When our index assigns murder 7,300 days and petit larceny 45 days, it asserts a specific exchange rate: one murder produces the same aggregate harm as 162 petit larcenies. Is this true? Is it useful? Is it dangerous?

The composite-indicator literature provides a precise vocabulary for this problem. Nardo et al. (2008) distinguish between *weights as importance coefficients* (how much each component "matters") and *weights as trade-offs* (how much of one component compensates for a unit of another). In linear aggregation — the method used by every published CHI — weights function as trade-offs, not importance coefficients. The CHI does not say "murder is 162 times more important than petit larceny." It says "a reduction of one murder can be fully compensated by an increase of 162 petit larcenies, and the index will show no change in total harm." This is the property of *full compensability*, and it is a strong assumption that no CHI paper has acknowledged.

Ravallion (2012) calls composite indices that lack theoretical guidance on design "mashup indices" and subjects them to four tests: conceptual clarity about what is being measured, transparency about the embedded marginal rates of substitution, robustness of rankings to alternative specifications, and policy relevance beyond what the component indicators already provide. The CHI fails the second test by default — no published CHI states its implied trade-off ratios — and has only begun to address the third.

### The Social Choice Foundation

The theoretical problem the CHI solves — aggregating heterogeneous values into a social ordering — is the problem of social choice theory. Arrow's (1951) impossibility theorem establishes that no aggregation procedure can simultaneously satisfy a set of mild conditions (Pareto efficiency, non-dictatorship, independence of irrelevant alternatives, unrestricted domain). This result applies directly to any attempt to rank places, offenders, or time periods by total harm: there is no aggregation rule that is simultaneously fair, consistent, and responsive to all dimensions of harm.

But Sen's (1999) resolution of the impossibility provides the philosophical foundation for the CHI enterprise — a foundation the CHI literature has never claimed. Sen showed that Arrow's impossibility arises from *informational poverty*: restricting social choice to voting-type rules that exclude interpersonal comparisons of well-being. Even *partial* interpersonal comparability — not full cardinal measurement, but enough to say that a murder victim's loss exceeds a shoplifting victim's loss — is sufficient to break the impossibility and produce a consistent social ordering.

This is exactly what the CHI does. Sentencing ranges are a legislatively enacted system of partial comparability: they assert that some crimes are more serious than others, and they quantify the degree of difference, without claiming to measure harm with the precision of a physical instrument. The statutory midpoint of the sentencing range for Murder 2nd (7,300 days) is not an exact measurement of the harm caused by a murder. It is a democratic, imperfect, but sufficient basis for comparing the severity of murder to the severity of petit larceny — and for making practical allocation decisions on that basis.

No CHI paper has made this connection to social choice theory. The result is that the CHI's theoretical justification rests entirely on Sherman et al.'s (2016) five-criteria test (democratic, reliable, affordable, valid, operational) — a pragmatic checklist that works well for practitioners but provides no defense against the deeper objection that incommensurable harms cannot share a cardinal scale. Sen provides that defense. The philosophical question is not whether crime harms are perfectly commensurable — they are not — but whether there exists a sufficient basis for partial comparison. Legislative sentencing ranges, enacted through democratic processes that implicitly rank the severity of different criminal acts, constitute exactly such a basis.

### The Behavioral Consequences of Measurement

The third tradition this paper engages is the institutional sociology of measurement — what happens when a metric is adopted by an organization.

Bevan and Hood (2006) documented the consequences of target-based governance in the English NHS. When hospitals were measured on waiting times, they gamed the metric: reclassifying trolleys as beds, queuing ambulances outside emergency departments, cancelling follow-up appointments to meet new-patient targets (resulting in 25 patients losing their vision). When ambulance trusts were measured on 8-minute response times, a third of them reclassified response times between 8 and 9 minutes as under 8 minutes. Goodhart's Law — "any observed statistical regularity will tend to collapse once pressure is placed on it for control purposes" — applied with devastating precision.

The CHI has not been subjected to this critique. If a CHI is used to allocate patrol resources to the highest-harm blocks, the behavioral dynamics Bevan and Hood identify will apply. Officers and analysts will attend to what the index measures (the 10 target offense categories) and neglect what it does not (fear of crime, disorder, emerging crime types). Recording practices may shift at blocks near the targeting threshold. Commanders may discover that reclassifying a felony assault as a misdemeanor assault reduces a block's CHI by over 1,400 days — the difference between 1,643 (D Violent felony) and 183 (A misdemeanor). These are not hypothetical concerns; they are the predictable consequences of any metric used for resource allocation (Jerven, 2013; Maguire, 2007).

The mitigation is not to avoid measurement — police departments already allocate resources based on crime counts, which are equally gameable and far less informative. The mitigation is transparency about what the CHI captures and what it misses, the use of the CHI as a *targeting tool* (directing where to investigate) rather than an *accountability metric* (measuring how well police performed), and institutional safeguards against recording manipulation.

Bevan and Hood provide the formal vocabulary for this discussion. They define Ω as the total performance domain, α as the target domain (what is measured), and β as everything outside it. The CHI's α consists of 10 offense categories weighted by sentencing midpoints. Its β includes unreported crime, fear, disorder, police-community relations, and the quality of investigations. The risk of synecdoche — taking α to stand for Ω — is real and should be explicitly acknowledged.

### What This Paper Does

We construct a Crime Harm Index for New York City that makes four contributions:

**First**, we introduce a statutory-midpoint weighting approach that uses the midpoint of New York State Penal Law sentencing ranges as CHI weights. This avoids the equity concerns of actual-sentencing indices (Lewis, Pina-Sánchez & Birks, 2024), the ceiling effects of maximum-sentence indices (Mitchell, 2019), and the unavailability of sentencing-council guidelines in U.S. jurisdictions (Sherman et al., 2016). The approach is generalizable to any U.S. state with structured sentencing — which is most of them.

**Second**, we aggregate the CHI to the physical block level — the finest operational grain for place-based intervention — and demonstrate the empirical conditions under which harm-weighting produces meaningfully different targeting decisions than equal-weight counting. Following Blumstein (1974), we compute the inter-crime-type correlation matrix across NYC blocks and show that correlations fall far below the ~0.96 threshold at which weighting becomes irrelevant. Following Weinborn et al. (2017), we show that harm is substantially more concentrated than volume, with only partial overlap between the highest-harm and highest-count blocks.

**Third**, we subject the index to the full battery of composite-indicator robustness tests that the OECD Handbook (Nardo et al., 2008) prescribes and that no prior CHI has performed: Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis across weight specifications, ranking confidence intervals, compensability testing (linear vs. geometric vs. lexicographic aggregation), and decomposition of top-harm blocks by offense category. We publish the embedded trade-off ratios that Ravallion (2012) and Saltelli (2007) require for any composite index claiming to guide resource allocation.

**Fourth**, we frame the CHI explicitly as an act of commensuration — following Espeland and Stevens (1998) — and as an exercise in partial comparability — following Sen (1999). We argue that the theoretical justification for any CHI is not that sentencing days perfectly measure harm, but that they provide a sufficient basis for practical targeting in the sense that Sen's framework requires: enough interpersonal comparability to break Arrow's impossibility and produce a consistent (if imperfect) ordering of places by severity of harm. We discuss the behavioral risks of institutionalizing such an index — following Bevan and Hood (2006) — and recommend safeguards.

### What This Paper Argues

The core argument is that the Crime Harm Index is a *composite indicator* in the precise technical sense of the OECD Handbook, and should be constructed, tested, and reported accordingly. The CHI literature's failure to engage with the composite-indicator tradition has left it methodologically underdeveloped: no published CHI performs sensitivity analysis, reports ranking confidence intervals, tests compensability, publishes trade-off ratios, or validates against external criteria. These are not optional embellishments. They are the minimum standard for any composite index used to guide consequential decisions (Saltelli, 2007).

At the same time, the composite-indicator literature has never been applied to a harm-weighted crime index — missing an application that raises distinctive theoretical questions about commensuration, democratic legitimacy, and the behavioral consequences of measurement. The CHI is an unusual composite: its weights are not statistical artifacts (like PCA loadings) or expert opinions (like budget allocations) but *democratically enacted legislative judgments* about the relative severity of criminal acts. This gives the CHI a claim to legitimacy that most composite indicators lack — and it makes the CHI a particularly interesting case for the commensurability debate, because the commensuration is performed not by analysts but by the legislature, through the criminal law itself.

We conclude that the CHI, properly constructed and transparently reported, is a defensible tool for place-based targeting — not because it perfectly measures harm, but because it provides a sufficient basis for partial comparison in a domain where the alternative (counting all crimes equally) is demonstrably worse. The strongest objection to the CHI is not that it is imperfect, but that it might be treated as if it were perfect. The antidote is the transparency this paper provides: the trade-off ratios it embeds, the sensitivity of its rankings to specification choices, the offense categories that drive its targeting decisions, and the dimensions of public safety it does not capture.

### Why This Matters Beyond Criminology

This paper is addressed to three audiences:

**For criminologists and policing practitioners**, it provides the most rigorous validation of a CHI to date, with methods that can be replicated in any jurisdiction with structured sentencing. The robustness tests answer the practical question: *can we trust this index to guide targeting?*

**For composite-indicator methodologists**, it provides a novel application domain — crime harm — that raises distinctive questions about democratic legitimacy (weights set by legislatures, not analysts), behavioral consequences (Goodhart's Law applied to policing), and the commensuration of qualitatively different harms (murder vs. theft). The CHI is an unusually transparent composite because its weights have explicit statutory origins and its trade-offs are directly interpretable in physical units (days of imprisonment).

**For scholars of measurement, commensurability, and social choice**, it provides a worked example of Sen's partial comparability in an operational context. The CHI demonstrates that the social choice problem is not merely theoretical: every police department that publishes crime statistics is making an implicit aggregation choice (equal weights), and the question is whether to make that choice explicit, democratic, and testable. The CHI's answer is yes — not because commensuration across qualitatively different harms is philosophically unproblematic, but because the alternative is a measurement regime that counts a murder and a shoplifting as the same thing, and allocates resources accordingly.
