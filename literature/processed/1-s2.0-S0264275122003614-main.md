A B S T R A C T    

Since Louis Thurstone, there have been nearly 100 years of attempts to compare and evaluate the severities of  different crime types. Further research has been recently conducted, following methods such as the Cambridge  Crime Harm Index (CCHI), which evaluates the harmful impact of crime on the society and identifies the areas in  a city with truly high crime harm by weighting the more serious crimes. Although this index facilitates informed  urban policy, its effectiveness in settings such as that of Japan, in which the rate of violent crimes, including gun  crime, is low, and where crime occurrence is generally infrequent, remains unclear. Here, the Japanese CHI was  analyzed by examining the criminal offense data of 77 jurisdictions in central Tokyo in 2010–2019. The pre-  weighted counts and post-weighted harm were compared to examine the correlations and time-series changes  between them, as well as their differences via different indicators, such as the Gini coefficient and Moran's I  statistic. Additionally, space-time scans and principal component regressions were employed to determine the  specific characteristics of the jurisdictions where harm accumulates. The result indicated that although the  temporal and spatial variability of harm was higher than that of its counts, the tendency of high-harm jurisdictions to cluster was lower. Further, the implications of our findings, particularly regarding the socioeconomic  factors that were revealed by the regression analysis, to urban policymakers were also discussed.   

1. Introduction 

Public safety or freedom from crime is a significant indicator for  assessing the livability of a city. For example, the most recent Safe Cities  Index 2021, which is published by the  Economist Intelligence Unit  (2021) (the research arm of the Economist), ranked 60 cities worldwide  from four perspectives. One such perspective is personal security, which  is evaluated based on the prevalence of different crime types and other  indicators of public safety. Further, digital crime maps have been  developed in many developed countries, ensuring the elucidation of  local crime situations. Based on this information, the safety of a city can  be confirmed. 

Thus, the issue of crime is critical to urban dwellers, although the  evaluation of public safety is still challenging probably because crime is 

the aggregation of different phenomena and consists of several categories of severity, from shoplifting to murder. To determine the rates of  different crime types in a given area, different rates must be compared.  The occurrence of crime in a region is generally expressed as an index,  which simply sums up all the major crimes (statistically known as  “crimes total”). Generally, cities are also ranked according to this  “crimes total” index. 

Although the advantage of such simple aggregation is that only one  indicator is required, there is also a serious disadvantage, such that the  overall result can be easily influenced by low-severity, high-frequency  crimes. In extreme cases, a city with a very high level of petty crimes  may be considered less safe than one with a high level of serious crimes.  To mitigate this, criminologists and police practitioners have introduced  the  “crime harm”  phenomenon. According to the Cambridge Crime

For over 50 years, scholars have been attempting to develop a  measure for juvenile delinquency with a focus on the “quality” rather  than “quantity” of the crime. Louis Thurstone, famous for his work on  the multifactor theory of intelligence, assessed the relative severities of  crimes by comparing pairs of different crime types (Thurstone, 1927).  Subsequently, Thorsten Sellin and Marvin Wolfang of the Center for  Studies in Criminology and Criminal Law at the University of Pennsylvania conducted a study on juvenile delinquency. They employed  judges, police officers, and college students and required them to rate  the severity of each delinquency after presenting a list of different offenses to them (Sellin & Wolfgang, 1964). Afterward, Wolfgang et al.  (1985) developed a “severity” score table with scores ranging from 0.2  for “A person under 16 years old plays hooky from school” to 72.1 for “A  person plants a bomb in a public building. The bomb explodes, and 20  people are killed.”4  Akman et al. (1967), Normandeau (1966), Rossi  et al. (1974), and Velez-Diaz and Megargee (1970) similarly attempted  to assess the severities of crimes. More recently, Ignatans and Pease  (2016) assessed crime severity by surveying crime victims and exam-

ining the changes they exhibit over time. Some scholars (e.g., Brand &  Price, 2000; Cohen et al., 1994; Heeks et al., 2018; McCollister et al.,  2010; Miller, 1996) have also attempted to convert the damages caused  by crime into economic values, as well as evaluate the cost of judicial  procedures, the cost of recovery, etc. 

Although these indicators allow the assessment of different crime  harm aspects, they require social surveys and secondary research and  therefore enormous resources when applied to a new city. Conversely,  CCHI is a simple indicator that can be scored simply by referring to the  sentencing guidelines. In this regard, CCHI passes the cost test (it can be  implemented without an additional cost), as stated by Sherman et al.  (2016), as well as the democracy test (it reflects the process adopted by  democratic governments; i.e., it reflects the people's will) and reliability  test (it can be applied consistently to different units of analysis, such as  time, place, and person, as well as produce the same results for the same  levels of harm). Owing to the foregoing benefits, as well as efforts to  spread the index phenomenon, it has spread to cities worldwide and has  been employed in the United States (Fenimore, 2019; Mitchell, 2019),  Australia (House & Neyroud, 2018; Ransley et al., 2018), New Zealand  (Curtis-Ham & Walton, 2018), Denmark (Andersen & Mueller-Johnson,  2018), and Sweden (K¨arrholm et al., 2020); these countries have  attempted to develop their CHI variants.5 There are also experimental  studies, such as whether police patrols (Ariel et al., 2016;  Mitchell,  2019) and court-ordered 24-month injunctions (Carr et al., 2017) reduce  harm, with practical applications. Curtis-Ham and Walton  (2017) compared maps from the Priority Locations Index (PLI), which  considers demographics such as district income, education level, and  employment status, as well as the prevalence of certain crimes, at the  New Zealand census tabulation district level, with CHI. They observed  that PLI better assessed the risk of certain residential communities,  whereas CHI could assess the risk of a wider range of community types.  Fenimore (2019)  employed systems, including CHI according to the  British sentencing guideline (CCHI), CHI according to the American  federal sentencing guideline, and Wolfgang's (1985) severity score to  weigh crimes, to compare the crime density map of Washington D.C.  with raw crime density maps; they observed that both density distribution maps, which were based on a simple count or severity scores,  were similar and exhibited high levels in the city center, although the  distribution maps of the harm attributable to the two CHI types revealed  that other areas besides and away from the city center also faced risks. Thus, only a few studies (e.g.,  Curtis-Ham & Walton, 2017) have focused on the geographical distri-

bution of harm scores; most studies are limited to visual comparisons,  such as the detection of harm clusters and the detailed analyses of their  socioeconomic characteristics. 

One issue that has not been clarified regarding the representation of  criminal harm is the nature of harm output in settings where the crime  count is generally low. There might be no difference between simple  incidence and harm representations in settings where the crime rate is  generally low even with a serious crime-focused approach, such as CHI. This is obvious that the way CHI is calculated. Regarding the  relationship between the frequency and weight of harm, the cumulative  impact of minor crimes becomes greater regardless of their weight as the  frequency of serious crimes approaches zero, and this result might  correspond to the simple expression obtained via crime counts. While  simulations can confirm how often this happens, policy implications  should be obtained by examining the harm output under the actual  crime levels and social conditions of the existing countries and regions.

3.2. Constructing harm weight and calculating score 

Here, we attempted to replicate the CCHI based on the Japanese  Penal Code.8 The harm weight values in CCHI are set at the lower limit  of the sentence (number of incarceration days), as specified in the  article. The harm weight values regarding financial penalties are set by  calculating the number of working days that would be required to earn a  specified amount of money, such as a fine, based on minimum wage. In  the Japanese Penal Code, the lower limit of the sentence is not given for  some crimes, particularly those for which a heavy penalty is expected;  rather, an upper limit is given. Additionally, only the upper limit is given  for fines. Therefore, the following rules were employed to set the weight  values:  

1. If the lowest imprisonment term was specified, it would be converted  into a day's count and set as a score.  

2. For those with unspecified lowest terms, an available reference to a  fine or minor fine would be employed. Here, the assumed working  hours were seven per day.

4.2. Gini coefficient and Moran's I statistic 

Table 6 shows the Gini coefficients and global Moran's I statistic for  the count and harm scores. The Gini coefficients for the count and harm,  which measure the degree of unevenness in the distribution regardless of  the geographic proximity, are moderate for the count and harm scores. Conversely, considering Moran's I statistic, the  degree of geographic clustering demonstrated a small (though statistically significant) spatial autocorrelation, starting from 2010 for the  count and the harm scores; it decreased over time. Furthermore, the  spatial autocorrelation value became insignificant in the three most  recent years for the harm score, indicating that it exhibited a different  trend from that of the Gini coefficient. The geographic distribution of  harm was not geographically concentrated in the high-scoring jurisdictions, although the distribution of harm in certain jurisdictions exhibited  unevenness; rather, they were dispersed, and this tendency was more  evident in more recent years. 

4.3. Space-time clusters 

Fig. 4 shows the aggregation range and duration of the statistically  significant clusters, which were detected via the space-time permutation  scan statistic of each count and harm score. Four and seven clusters were  detected for the prime count and harm score, respectively, with the  latter detecting several clusters that were spatially smaller than the  former. The northwestern part of the map revealed that the area formed  a single spatio-temporal cluster in the count, while multiple clusters  were detected in the harm. This result indicates that the geographical  concentration of harm was more transient than that of the count. 

Regarding the specific areas exhibiting the spatio-temporal clusters,  the eastern, northwestern, and central clusters were common to both  counts and harm, although their details were different. For example, the  spatio-temporal cluster by harm included Shinjuku (id: 34) and Ikebukuro (id: 47) jurisdictions, as well as captured the downtown area where 

the level of crime was high. Conversely, for harm, we observed clusters, such as Sugamo (id:  46), Takinogawa (id: 49), Oji (id: 50), and Akabane (id: 51), which  largely correspond to Kita-ku. Although Kita-ku is adjacent to Adachi-  ku, it exhibits good accessibility to the city center owing to the abundant main railroad lines; thus, it could be considered an area close to the  city center in terms of time distance.

Regarding spatio-temporal clustering, the harm clusters appeared  and disappeared on a small scale and within a short period compared  with the count clusters, indicating that they were transient. In this sense, the  harm cluster can be considered a very accurate reflection of the actual  conditions. However, only 2018–2019 were judged as clusters with  Shinjuku jurisdiction, and these clusters included many low-harm jurisdictions. This could be considered a limitation of the cylindrical scan. Conversely, PCR did not reveal any major difference between the two  results. However, we cautiously matched the results of the spatio-  temporal cluster analysis because of the constraints of the analysis  since the predictions were based on the independent variables that were  observed at different periods from 2010 to 2019 for the ten-year accumulation of counts/harm. Since most of the information regarding the  census is available only over a long period (e.g., five years), it was  challenging to perform yearly regression analyses. We could not find any existing study that examined the factors  associated with counts and harm scores respectively, but Curtis-Ham 

and Walton (2017) did conduct a regression analysis for harms alone.  They observed that overall indicators such as relative deprivation and  economic disadvantage positively affected it, while population and the  urban/rural classification (the more urban, the lower the value) negatively affected it (i.e., the more urban, the higher the harm). Compared  with this study, the results are similar in that pc3 negatively affected  harm;  however,  the  other  results  were  not  necessarily  consistent.  Comparing the crime frequency between New Zealand and Tokyo's 23  wards, the target of Curtis-Ham and Walton (2017), we find that theft is  1.6 times higher in Tokyo's 23 wards, while murders and robberies are  3.4 times and 12.3 times higher in New Zealand, respectively. However,  the difference in the results may be due to the difference in the scale of  analysis, i.e., country-level analysis versus city-level analysis, rather  than the difference in the frequency of crime. Fenimore (2019) and  Weinborn et al. (2017) did not examine the factors influencing harm, but  they considered the possibility that lack of guardianship or natural  surveillance might be related to harm given the presence of harm distribution outside densely populated urban centers. The results of the  current analysis show that pc2, which is negatively associated with  daytime population as a proxy for guardianship, has a negative effect on  both count and harm. Although our results are consistent with their  speculation, our analysis was only at the police jurisdiction level,  meaning that street-level analysis is necessary to examine the effects of  micro factors such as guardianship. 

Next, the implications of this study for urban and public safety policies are discussed. First, for the police, who are the primary actors in  crime control in Japan, the results of this study can be employed as a  reference  for  reallocating resources, such  as officers  and budgets.  Particularly, the Tokyo Metropolitan Police Department (MPD), which  oversees the district police departments, should refer to this information. The MPD's latest outlook document lists issues intended to optimize  operations, such as “flexible organization” and “streamlining,” in addition to addressing new security issues other than conventional street

crime (Metropolitan Police Department, 2020). In its latest summary of  the crime situation, the National Police Agency, which oversees prefectural and metropolitan police departments nationwide, acknowledges that there are issues in the security situation that cannot be  grasped from conventional indicators, such as  “the total number of  recognized criminal offenses has been continuously decreasing, mainly  for offense types and modus operandi that account for a large proportion  of the total number, but there are increasing trends for some offenses  and other situations that are not necessarily captured from the trends of  recognized cases” (National Police Agency, 2022). By introducing the  concept of harm, identifying areas where crimes detrimental to the  public interest are frequent was possible. Although the results of this  study did not indicate that harm could change our perception of the  public safety situation, they can motivate us to change our priorities in  allocating resources. However, based on the results of this study, the  districts that emerge by focusing on both counts and harm scores are not  entirely different from those in the past. Rather, it is possible to optimize  resources and maximize returns by  “investing”  more than before in  districts with high counts and even high harm scores. The fact that  harm's Gini coefficient was stable also strengthens the persuasiveness of  this argument. Such an argument is only possible by examining aspects  of harm in a low crime frequency situation. 

The  same  can  be  said  for  policymakers  of  local  governments.  Regarding local governments, different scales of information are available to the Tokyo Metropolitan Government, which is the autonomous  body that controls these wards and the ward office, which is the basic  autonomous body governing each of the 23 wards. In terms of autonomous bodies, this study avails them the opportunity to pause and  consider the policy priorities between jurisdictions in their wards and  between wards for the Tokyo Metropolitan Government. Regarding the  jurisdictions and wards with higher harm, the study offers recommendations on how to improve welfare and housing policies, as well as  determine the volunteer crime-prevention groups in the neighborhood  that must be supported by the life safety department. The key here is not  to focus on manipulating specific variables, such as the foreign population or mid- and high-rise rental housing; it is to consider jurisdictions  and wards with such characteristics and consider policies that can  support them. 

Thus, one challenge with employing this indicator in policymaking is  that its value is not inherently stable. As observed with the standard  deviations and the spatio-temporal clusters, harm gives a large weight to  low-frequency but severe crimes, and since such crimes do not necessarily occur consistently in the same jurisdiction, the values would  fluctuate among jurisdictions, resulting in a high degree of spatio-  temporal variability. Therefore, as mentioned in the introduction, we  could not readily conclude that a jurisdiction exhibited a structure that  truly generated harm even by examining harm in units of several months  or years. Thus, it is still necessary to consider the accumulation of harm  over a period of several years, as in this study, as well as to consider the  bare crime count. 

It is of public interest to make information about harm available to  residents, and this information might help them select their residences.  However, this might stigmatize certain communities. In Japan, the  identification of criminal offenses and counts are made public, but it is  presumed that the dissemination of harm is more likely to cause anxiety  about public safety than counts. Further, the concept of harm itself, as  well as its calculation, must be fully explained, and public entities must  be cautious in disseminating them.

Although there were  no fundamental differences between the crime counts and harm, we  observed micro differences and some factors with implications for  formulating urban policies. 

Although some relevant findings were obtained regarding the dif-

ference between count and harm in low-frequent crime situations, which  is the main focus of this study, caution must be exercised when viewing  this harm because the weighing of crime based on the criminal law  provisions of each country's sentence only ranks the crimes relative to  each other and does not perfectly quantify the damage caused by the  crime. Therefore, it is challenging to compare the absolute value of harm  scores among countries. Indeed, the story of harm in Japan is different  from that in a society where gun ownership and use are broadly  permitted by law. Harm in each country can only be employed to  determine the areas that are relatively more “serious” or “better.” 

We can also identify the disadvantages of harm by relying solely on  quantitative punishment. Indeed, as reported by Sherman et al. (2016),  although CCHI is a simple and reliable method, as indicated by the  weights listed in Table 3, the same weight values are assigned to crimes  that are essentially different. It would be unnatural for affected citizens  to imagine that these types of crimes would cause the same damage.  Rather, it is reasonable to assume that different types of crimes would  inflict different magnitudes of damage if we subdivide the damage by  psychological, physical, economic, and other dimensions. For example,  Greenfield and Paoli (2013) proposed dimensions, such as functional  integrity, material interest, reputation, and privacy, with particular  attention to the harm caused by crimes committed by organizations,  such as corporations. For future research on crime harm, we recommend  the consideration of indicators based on dynamic indicators that reflect  the values of the general public and economic agents within the period,  as well as on stable and static criteria, such as quantitative punishment.
