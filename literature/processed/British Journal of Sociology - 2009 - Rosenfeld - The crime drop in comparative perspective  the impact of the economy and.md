# The crime drop in comparative perspective: the impact of the economy and imprisonment on American and European burglary rates

Richard Rosenfeld and Steven F. Messner

Abstract

Inﬂuential statements on recent American crime reductions maintain that the crime drop was conﬁned to the USA.Yet other research has revealed comparable crime decreases in Europe.We suggest that the USA and European crime declines occurred in tandem because they were both brought about by upturns in the economy. In light of US research showing crime reductions resulting from growth in imprisonment, we also examine the possibility that rising imprisonment rates reduced European crime rates. We test these hypotheses in a pooled crosssectional time-series analysis of burglary rates in the USA and nine European nations between 1993 and 2006. The results indicate that burglary declines in the US and Europe were associated with rising consumer conﬁdence. By contrast, imprisonment appears to be signiﬁcantly related to burglary rates only after unusual policy interventions, such as Italy’s 2006 clemency measure that dramatically reduced the size of its prison population. We interpret these ﬁndings as reﬂecting the structural similarity and economic integration of the world’s developed nations and the uneven convergence in US and European punishment policies.

Keywords: Crime drop; burglary rates; economy; imprisonment

A dramatic drop in violent and property crime occurred in the USA during the 1990s, leading one analyst to herald the reduction ‘the great American crime decline’ (Zimring 2006a).Whether crime rates were falling elsewhere in the world, and for the same reasons, are questions that have been largely absent from academic and public commentary in the USA. In this paper we confront the challenge of explaining cross-national similarities in recent crime trends by examining burglary rates in the USA and nine European nations between 1993 and 2006. We hypothesize that the same economic and social conditions associated with the American crime decline, notably economic expansion and rising imprisonment rates, may have exerted similar inﬂuences in developed European societies. The results of a pooled cross-sectional time-series analysis of year-to-year change in burglary rates conﬁrm the economic hypothesis: As in the USA, burglary rates in European nations fell during the period in response to upward swings in the business cycle as indicated by rising consumer conﬁdence. Rising imprisonment rates are also signiﬁcantly associated with burglary declines, but this result largely reﬂects the substantial reduction in Italy’s prison population in 2006. Our ﬁndings highlight the fundamental economic integration of the world’s developed nations,as well as the impact of national variation in punishment policies.

After rising sharply during the 1970s and 1980s, rates of serious violent and property crime in the USA fell during the 1990s to levels not seen since the 1960s. Police-recorded homicide, robbery, and burglary rates decreased by more than 40 per cent from 1993 to 2000 (Rosenfeld 2004).2 Victim surveys reveal even sharper declines in non-lethal assaults, robberies, and property crimes.3 The crime drop was wide as well as deep, affecting nearly all major demographic groups and regions of the country,and both small and large cities (Zimring 2006a). ### Broad Assessments

Scholarly accounts of the 1990s American crime drop differ from one another with respect to its putative causes but converge in their singular focus on the USA. Blumstein and Wallman (2000, 2006) compiled papers from leading crime researchers on the contribution to the crime drop of restricted ﬁrearm availability, shrinking drug markets, changes in policing, prison expansion, the economy, and demographic change. The volume reaches no deﬁnitive conclusions regarding the relative importance of these factors, but some (e.g., declining demand for crack and other hard drugs, rising wages for younger workers) emerge as stronger contributors to the crime decline than do others (e.g., policing innovations) (see Rosenfeld 2004, for a similar assessment). Levitt’s (2004) research review concludes that declining unemployment, policing innovations, demographic change, changes in gun laws, and increased use of capital punishment had little or no impact on the crime decline. He argues that the reductions resulted instead from rising imprisonment rates, growth in police forces, shrinking crack markets, and the legalization of abortion during the 1970s. Levitt (2004: 166) concludes that ‘the drop in U. S. crime appears to be unusual among countries of the world’.

Zimring (2006a) documents the breadth, magnitude, and longevity of the American crime drop, compares it with Canadian trends, and addresses the special case of NewYork City,in which crime rates fell even more dramatically than in the nation at large. He rejects several explanations of the US crime reductions largely because they cannot explain similar declines in Canada. Canadian imprisonment rates did not increase during the 1990s, and Canada did not experience the crack epidemic that took hold in US cities during the 1980s and so could not have beneﬁted from crack’s demise. Nor did the Canadian economy, according to Zimring, undergo the sustained boom that occurred in the USA. Zimring (2006a) concludes, somewhat tentatively, that the ageing of the population and a common ‘cyclical dynamic’ were the only factors linking the American and Canadian crime declines.

As for New York City, where homicide rates dropped by roughly threequarters during the 1990s, Zimring (2006a) attributes part of the reduction to policing innovations associated with New York’s Compstat initiative and part to conditions New York shared with other US cities, including an expanding national economy and rising levels of imprisonment (cf. Karmen 2000; Messner et al. 2007; Rosenfeld, Fornango and Rengifo 2007).

### Recent research

In contrast with the broad and,except for Levitt (2004),inconclusive discursive evaluations of the American crime drop, recent empirical research on US crime trends tells a clearer causal story: Crime rates fall with economic expansion and growth in imprisonment. Some studies also attribute crime rate changes to shifts in the age composition of the population, ﬂuctuations in the number of police ofﬁcers, and abortion policy changes (Levitt 2004), but these results are less consistent across studies than those on the economy and imprisonment.

### The economy

Several recent studies conclude that crime rates fell with the expansion of the US economy during the 1990s. In a panel study of US states between 1986 and 2000 Arvanites and Deﬁna (2006) ﬁnd that reductions in rates of robbery and property crime resulted from real growth in Gross Domestic Product (GDP) measured at the state level. Rosenfeld and Fornango (2007) ﬁnd a sizable and robust association between collective perceptions of economic conditions, as reﬂected in a measure of ‘consumer sentiment’, and changes in robbery and property crime rates between 1970 and 2003 in the four major US census regions. They conclude that collective perceptions of economic improvement accounted for as much as one-quarter to one-half of the decline in robbery and property crime during the 1990s.

Neither the Arvanites and Deﬁna (2006) nor the Rosenfeld and Fornango (2007) studies ﬁnds a signiﬁcant impact of changing rates of unemployment on crime trends (see, also, Baumer 2008; I˙mrohorog˘lu, Merlo and Rupert 2004). The unemployment rate is by far the economic indicator of choice in research on economic conditions and crime rates. Studies of the temporal relationship between unemployment and crime have produced mixed results, with some studies ﬁnding the expected positive effect, some no effect, and others ﬁnding a negative effect, usually interpreted as meaning that rising unemployment cuts crime by reducing criminal opportunities and target attractiveness (Cantor and Land 1985; Fielding, Clarke, and Witt 2000; Paternoster and Bushway 2001). None the less, two studies ﬁnd a signiﬁcant positive temporal relationship between unemployment and crime rates in the USA during the period encompassing the crime drop (Gould, Weinberg, and Mustard 2002; Raphael and Winter-Ebmer 2001).

It appears, then, that the US economic expansion of the 1990s, which reduced unemployment, increased real GDP, and improved collective perceptions of economic conditions, also contributed to the crime drop. ### Imprisonment

The US imprisonment rate increased 260 per cent between 1980 and 2006. With a combined prison and jail population of 755 inmates per 100,000 population in 2006, the USA has by far the highest incarceration rate in the world.4 It would be surprising if incarceration levels and growth rates this large had no impact on crime rates. A consistent result of longitudinal studies is that increases in incarceration are associated with reductions in crime (Levitt 1996; Liedka, Piehl, and Useem 2006; Marvell and Moody 1994; Rosenfeld and Fornango 2007). Some uncertainty remains over how to identify the effect of incarceration on crime, given that rising crime rates tend to result in rising incarceration rates as offenders are processed through the criminal justice system (Spelman 2008). But that implies that current estimates of the effect of incarceration on crime, if anything, probably understate the true effect.5

### The European crime drop

As noted, victimization rates in Europe as reﬂected in surveys fell during the past decade after having risen through the early 1990s, lagging a few years behind the American crime drop (van Dijk, Kesteren and Smit 2007; see, also, Nieuwbeerta 2002).The deﬁnitional and measurement uniformity in the ICVS is an important advantage for assessing comparative crime trends over police recorded data, which are subject to variation across nations in offence deﬁnitions, classiﬁcation rules, and recording procedures. None the less, a crossnational comparison conducted by Home Ofﬁce researchers also shows reductions in police-recorded burglaries and motor vehicle thefts in most European nations between 1997 and 2001 (Barclay and Tavares 2003). The limited comparative assessments by some analysts of the US crime drop notwithstanding, then, the best available evidence does not support the assumption of American exceptionalism in recent crime trends. The crime drop, as well as the preceding crime climb, extended across the Atlantic.

Van Dijk (2006: 17) poses the critical theoretical question about these patterns: ‘What macro-level factors in Western societies can explain the upward trends in crime till 1995–2000 and the downward trends thereafter?’ We hypothesize that two salient factors that emerge from the research on the American crime drop – the economy and imprisonment – also underlie the European trends. Prison populations also grew in Europe during the 1990s and into the current century, albeit from far lower base rates than in the USA. Imprisonment rates increased modestly in some countries and sharply in others. For example, between 1992 and 2004 the number of prisoners per 100,000 population in France increased by 10 per cent and in Norway by 12 per cent. In a well known case of correctional reform, Finland cut its imprisonment rate by one-quarter during the 1990s, but it had climbed back to the level of the early 1990s by 2004. Scholarly accounts of prison expansion (e.g., Cavadino and Dignan 2006; Christie 2000; Downes 2001; Garland 2001; von Hirsch 1994) are largely silent on whether rising imprisonment may have reduced crime rates in Europe.7 An implicit assumption among comparative corrections policy analysts seems to be that, whatever the effect of imprisonment on crime in the USA, the sharply lower imprisonment rates in Europe could not result in crime reductions. We are not aware of any published research, however, that systematically compares imprisonment effects on aggregate crime rates in Europe and the USA and concur with Zimring (2006b) that comparative research is essential for evaluating explanations of crime or crime control policy in a single nation. Therefore, we evaluate the hypothesis based on the American research that prison growth contributed to the crime drop in both Europe and the USA.

### Mechanisms

Why should crime declines, economic improvements, and prison expansion have occurred at about the same time in Europe and the USA? Why should the economy or imprisonment affect aggregate crime rates in either the USA or other developed nations? The ﬁrst of these essential theoretical questions speaks to the structural similarities and interdependence of the world’s leading market economies. The second highlights the major incentives and controls that shape acquisitive criminal behaviour in developed societies.

### Controls

No such structural integration exists in crime control policy. European nations are generally considered to be less punitive than the USA. The USA has retained the death penalty long after most European nations abandoned it (Zimring 2003) and, as mentioned, European incarceration rates are much lower than those in the USA. But analysts of incarceration growth in Europe contend that it has resulted from many of the same forces at work in the USA: crime increases since the 1960s, a heightened sense of insecurity in the general population, the neoliberal turn in social policy to increase competitiveness by reducing social welfare costs, and a shift in punishment policy toward the bureaucratic management of ‘dangerous’ populations – racial and ethnic minorities in the USA, immigrants in Europe (Cavadino and Dignan 2006; Downes 2001; Garland 2001). The conﬂuence of these forces is especially evident in The Netherlands, where imprisonment rates increased more rapidly than in the USA during the 1990s (Barclay and Tavares 2003: 7, Table B; Downes 2007;Heiner 2005).US penal policy casts a large shadow over Europe, according to Nils Christie, by establishing ‘a new frame of reference’ for what is considered an acceptable level of incarceration in developed nations (quoted in Heiner 2005: 230). By the same logic, the Dutch experience may have facilitated incarceration growth elsewhere in Europe.

In summary, the interdependence of the US and European economies has produced a rough temporal correspondence in business cycles, and penal policies in the USA and Europe seem increasingly to be subject to similar political pressures with similar effects on the growth of prison populations. It remains to be explained how these convergent economic and incarceration trends might have contributed to crime reductions in Europe and the USA.

### Beneﬁts of conformity, costs of crime

Economic theories of crime predict that crime will decrease as the beneﬁts of lawful behaviour and the costs of criminal behaviour increase (Becker 1968; Ehrlich 1973). As the economy expands and employment and incomes grow, the beneﬁts of lawful behaviour should as well. The arguments in economic theory concerning the crime-reducing effects of economic expansion are quite similar to those in social control theories that emphasize ‘commitment’ and ‘stakes in conformity’ as bonds linking people to the conventional order (see especially Hirschi 1969).

When the economy contracts, conformity becomes less rewarding and income-producing criminal behaviour should increase, holding constant the costs of crime. For their part, the costs of crime grow with increases in the probability and severity of punishment. In most developed societies, the punishment of last – and increasingly ﬁrst – resort is imprisonment.

The crime drop would seem to be a textbook example of economic theory at work.Both the economy and the prisons expanded during the 1990s,increasing the beneﬁts of conformity and the costs of crime simultaneously.But economic thinking about crime tends to be highly abstract. It offers an ideal-typical picture of individuals rationally weighing costs and beneﬁts but does not specify the social contexts in which rational choices are made or the social processes that make some choices more ‘rational’ than others.

For example, economists generally read the evidence regarding the effect of imprisonment on crime as supporting deterrence models of punishment, but rarely indicate which model – speciﬁc or general deterrence – underlies the effect (Levitt 2001).9 Little evidence supports the speciﬁc-deterrent effect of imprisonment on subsequent offending (Nagin, Cullen and Jonson forthcoming), and it is difﬁcult to distinguish empirically a general deterrent effect of imprisonment on aggregate crime rates from an incapacitation effect (Blumstein, Cohen and Nagin 1978). Studies of imprisonment effects on crime in the USA typically assume that crime reductions result from some combination of general deterrence and incapacitation. Given the comparatively low levels of imprisonment in European nations, however, incapacitation is unlikely to be the primary social mechanism linking imprisonment to European crime reductions – except for abrupt policy changes that result in sharply higher or lower prison populations. In general, if recent growth in European prison populations has contributed to the crime drop, the mechanism is probably general deterrence.

Economic theories of crime are not much better at identifying the social mechanisms linking the economy to crime trends. Ethnographic research directs attention to underground markets, particularly markets for stolen goods, as a strategic social context that shapes opportunities and incentives for property crime (Sullivan 1989; Valentine 1978; Venkatesh 2006).Thieves must

### Data and methods

The outcome measure in our analysis is the annual burglary rate per 100,000 population.We focus on burglary because it is a serious property crime subject to fewer classiﬁcation and recording differences across nations than the extremely heterogeneous category of ‘theft’ or the violent offence of robbery, for which the requisite type and amount of ‘force’ may be deﬁned differently in different nations. We exclude motor vehicle theft because of the sizable fraction of auto thefts committed for ‘joy riding’ that have little economic motivation.

The burglary data are from Eurostat, which compiles crime data from national sources for EU member nations and selected other countries. An alternative source of European crime data is the European Sourcebook of Crime and Criminal Justice Statistics (ESCCJS). We do not use these data in the current study because the crime series in the most recent ESCCJS volume do not extend beyond 2003 (see http://www.europeansourcebook.org/). We compared the burglary rates derived from Eurostat and ESCCJS for those nations and years contained in both sources and found a high degree of consistency between the them (r = 0.99). In addition, we were able to conduct limited comparisons of the Eurostat burglary data with ICVS burglary data for six of the nations in our sample. In each instance the direction of change in the police-recorded and victimization burglary trends was the same and of roughly similar magnitude across the six nations.10 These cross-validation results lend conﬁdence in the use of the Eurostat crime data in our analysis.

The Eurostat burglary measure is limited to ‘domestic’ (i.e., residential) burglaries, but the data provided for the USA also include commercial burglaries. Throughout the period under investigation the national source for the US data, the Federal Bureau of Investigation’s Uniform Crime Reports (UCR), consistently classiﬁed about two-thirds of US burglaries as residential and the remainder as commercial. We therefore reduced the Eurostat burglary totals for the USA by one-third to make them comparable to those for the European nations in our sample. The population counts used to create the burglary rates are also from Eurostat. (Labels, descriptions, and sources for all of the variables included in the analysis are presented in Appendix A.)

Three economic indicators are included: GDP per capita in constant US dollars, the unemployment rate, and a measure of collective perceptions of economic conditions or ‘consumer conﬁdence’. The GDP and unemployment measures are from the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development (OECD). The consumer conﬁdence data for the European nations in the sample, compiled by Eurostat, are based on a composite index of items from representative monthly household surveys that ask persons to gauge their personal ﬁnancial situation and general economic conditions over the next 12 months. The US consumer conﬁdence data are based on the Reuters-University of Michigan’s Index of Consumer Sentiment, which also is derived from household surveys that ask representative samples of the adult population about their current ﬁnancial situation, general economic conditions, and expectations for the next 12 months. We use annualized consumer conﬁdence data from both sources in our analysis.

Although not identical, the US and European consumer conﬁdence surveys use very similar items to gauge subjective perceptions of personal and general economic conditions. An advantage of consumer conﬁdence over other economic indicators is that it reﬂects subjective perceptions of economic change unavailable from ‘objective’ measures such as unemployment or growth rates (see Rosenfeld and Fornango 2007). Because both the European and US conﬁdence measures ask respondents about their yearforward expectations, they contain information that cannot be obtained from measures based on present or past conditions. For this reason, we include both a contemporaneous and a one-year lagged measure of consumer conﬁdence in our analysis.11

The data on prisoners are from Eurostat and represent the number of prisoners per 100,000 population.The data for the USA include the jail as well as prison population to make them comparable to the European prisoner statistics. The USA is an extreme outlier on this measure, which has been transformed to its natural log to reduce skewness. In addition, we report separate results with the USA excluded from the sample.

### Measurement and models

Stationarity tests performed on the burglary time series revealed that these series are non-stationary for most of the nations included in the analysis. Regression analysis of non-stationary time series can produce spurious results (Raffalovich 1994).We therefore transformed the burglary rates to ﬁrst differences (yt - yt-1). Tests on the differenced series showed that all measures are difference stationary. Accordingly, all variables were ﬁrst-differenced for analysis.

We estimate ﬁxed effects panel models using ordinary least squares regression to explain variation in year-over-year change in burglary rates across the 10 nations. The nation ﬁxed effects condition the estimates on time invariant differences across nations not captured by the substantive variables in the model. Year ﬁxed effects condition the estimates on time-varying conditions common across nations. We also included linear and quadratic time trends in preliminary analyses.The quadratic trends were not signiﬁcant and have been excluded from the analyses shown.We retained the common linear trend and included nation-speciﬁc linear trends in the form of nation x year interaction terms. To control for serial correlation in the error terms and to capture additional sources of unmeasured heterogeneity in yearly change in burglary, our models include the burglary measure lagged one year. Diagnostic tests revealed that the error terms are homoskedastic. Because the USA is an extreme outlier on imprisonment and for comparison purposes, we report separate results with the USA omitted from the sample.

### Results

### Sensitivity analyses

We assessed the sensitivity of our results to model speciﬁcation by, ﬁrst, re-estimating the models with all non-signiﬁcant variables removed but retaining the lagged outcome, country and year ﬁxed effects, and trend indicators. The results for the consumer conﬁdence and imprisonment measures in the trimmed models are nearly identical to those in the full models. Second, we re-estimated the full models with the ﬁxed effects and trend indicators removed. This speciﬁcation reduces the magnitude and signiﬁcance of the lagged consumer conﬁdence coefﬁcient in the models estimated on both the full sample and the sample excluding the USA (b = -4.901 and -4.637, p = 0.105 and p = 0.152,respectively) but produces no other substantive changes in the estimated effects. Not surprisingly, model ﬁt is reduced appreciably, by about 50 per cent, with the ﬁxed effects omitted.

We also checked the sensitivity of our results to sample composition. We have shown that the effects of consumer conﬁdence and imprisonment on burglary are not an artifact of including the USA in the data but it is possible, especially in a small sample, that other nations inﬂuence these results in ways that the ﬁxed effects do not pick up.Therefore,we re-estimated the model with each nation omitted from the sample one at a time.

We found that the effect of the contemporaneous consumer conﬁdence measure is sensitive to the inclusion of Greece in the sample. With Greece omitted the contemporaneous effect of consumer conﬁdence is reduced appreciably and is no longer statistically signiﬁcant, but the lagged effect increases in magnitude by roughly 27 per cent and remains signiﬁcant (b = -13.375, p = 0.005).This result reﬂects the extreme volatility in consumer conﬁdence in Greece during the estimation period, particularly in the year 2000 when consumer conﬁdence rose by 37 per cent and 2002 when conﬁdence plummeted by 42 per cent over the previous year. In both instances conﬁdence reversed course and returned to previous levels in the next year. When either 2000 or 2002 is dropped from the estimation and Greece is retained in the sample, the effect of the contemporaneous measure of consumer conﬁdence is statistically signiﬁcant and similar in magnitude to that for the entire period.

It is difﬁcult to determine the reason for these abrupt reversals in consumer conﬁdence, although measurement error in the conﬁdence measure cannot be discounted. ### Conclusion

The crime drop in the USA has received extensive attention from American scholars, often viewed through the lens of ‘American exceptionalism’, and yet researchers have also observed that European nations have registered appreciable crime decreases over the past decade or so, including declines in burglary rates, the focus of the current study. We have proposed that the improvement in economic conditions associated with the US crime drop also contributed to burglary reductions in Europe, and we have evaluated this hypothesis in ﬁxed effects panel models of year-to-year change in burglary rates in the USA and nine European nations between 1993 and 2006. The results are generally consistent with our hypothesis: Collective perceptions of economic change, as reﬂected in a measure of consumer conﬁdence, are associated with changes in burglary rates, net of controls for several other substantive variables and unmeasured sources of variation within and between nations captured by common and nation-speciﬁc time trends and nation and year ﬁxed effects. Sensitivity analyses reveal that the results, especially the lagged effect of consumer conﬁdence on burglary, are reasonably robust against model speciﬁcation and sample composition. The effect of consumer conﬁdence is not trivial: A one standard deviation increase in consumer

conﬁdence is associated with an estimated burglary reduction the following year of about 5 per cent from the mean burglary rate.

We have also evaluated the effect of prison expansion on burglary rates, extending an American research subspecialty with European data. We ﬁnd that, in general, the growth in prisoners does not have a statistically signiﬁcant effect on burglary rates but that the substantial reduction in Italy’s prison population probably did contribute to a large rise in the burglary rate in 2006. This result must be regarded as tentative: a larger sample of nations or longer time period might make it possible to detect a general crime reduction effect from imprisonment growth. But the current analysis suggests that unusually large reductions in prison populations are necessary to produce discernible increases in burglary rates.

The current research is limited in several respects. Our analysis is restricted to a single, albeit important, offence type. Whether similar results obtain for property crimes other than burglary or for violent offences remains to be determined in future research. The sample of nations, covariates, and the time period under investigation are restricted by the availability of complete data on our main measures, which has prompted the use of rather extensive sensitivity assessments. More deﬁnitive conclusions require analyses based on a larger number of nations and longer time periods. We further caution that the hypothesized mechanisms linking the economy and imprisonment to changes in burglary rates – demand for stolen goods and general deterrence, respectively – are just that, hypotheses awaiting future investigation with data on the operation of underground markets and the perceptions of the imprisonment risk associated with speciﬁc types of offences.

Moreover, if our explanation linking burglary and consumer conﬁdence proves to be valid, important theoretical and policy questions arise. What are the processes operating at earlier stages in the causal sequence? Our analyses suggest that changes in GDP appear to have an indirect effect on changes in burglary rates via consumer conﬁdence, but the association between GDP and consumer conﬁdence is not particularly strong (r = 0.283), and as a result GDP as an indicator of the state of the economy contributes only modestly to the more proximate cause of changing burglary rates. The results also indicate that the effect of consumer conﬁdence operates independently of the other common indicators of economic conditions included in the analysis (unemployment rates, income inequality, and social welfare spending). If consumer sentiment is in fact an important determinant of levels of burglary, the relevant challenge from a policy standpoint is to identify those factors that inﬂuence consumer sentiment and that are amenable to manipulation.

In addition, future research on crime trends should incorporate other substantively important variables. One promising candidate is immigration,

which has been linked with crime in contrary ways in different national contexts. Increased immigration has been cited as a cause of recent increases in crime in Europe, although patterns vary by immigrant group (see Albrecht 2002). In contrast, researchers have suggested that rising levels of immigration have played an important role in reducing crime rates in the USA (Stowell et al. 2009; Sampson 2008). If the requisite data can be gathered, it would thus be instructive to examine systematically the extent to which, and the ways in which, changes in immigration also help account for trends in crime rates across various developed nations. Another fruitful area of inquiry is the potential impact of demographic processes other than those captured by the age structure and the sex ratio. For example, prior research in the USA has indicated that relatively large cohorts and those characterized by high levels of non-marital births have relatively high arrest rates for serious crimes (speciﬁcally homicide) throughout the life span (O’Brien, Stockard and Isaacson 1999). These ﬁndings suggest that convergence in contemporary trends in crime might reﬂect common fertility patterns during earlier periods.15

Finally, discrete events, such as Italy’s 2006 clemency measure, provide unique opportunities for natural experiments to identify deterrent or incapacitation effects of incarceration (cf.Barbarino and Mastrobuoni 2007).They also suggest that the prison expansion in Europe over the past two decades is not uniform across nations or policy regimes. We concur with Cavadino and Dignan’s (2006: 440) careful assessment of the convergence in American and European punishment policies:

Even the most general trends are not all going in the same direction everywhere. And with the US imprisonment rate far exceeding that of any other country, the differences remain massive. Some convergence, yes; homogenization – far from it.

We regard our analysis and results as promising but preliminary. If our results are replicated in future research they should bring constructive pressure on assessments of crime trends that are restricted to a single nation. Such studies can serve as useful points of departure, but by themselves they are likely to miss important conditions that explain crime rate changes across a broad class of societies, such as the developed nations that are the focus of the current research.16 Comparative research on the economy and crime assumes a special urgency during a global economic crisis in which consumer conﬁdence is plummeting in both the USA and Europe. The world’s developed nations share basic structural features, their economies are tethered, and their consumers respond similarly to ﬁnancial distress. Why should their crime trends be different?

under 2% each year. The yearly percentage changes were generated by obtaining the slope coefﬁcient from a regression of each nation’s annual burglary rate on a linear counter and dividing it by the nation’s 1993– 2006 mean burglary rate (and multiplying by 100).

- 13. Eurostat cautions that crime record-


ing methods can change, making it difﬁcult to interpret trends. Published volumes report annual data for such ‘breaks’ in the

crime series since 2000 (see http://epp. eurostat.ec.europa.eu/cache/ITY_OFFPUB/ KS-SF-08-019/EN/KS-SF-08-019-EN.PDF). (Last date accessed 13 May 2009.) For our sample, the only such break occurred in Italy’s burglary series in the year 2003. We re-estimated our main model with this observation removed and found no substantive differences in results from those reported in Table II.

- 14. Because the correlation between the

current and lagged consumer conﬁdence measures is small and non-signiﬁcant, their estimated effects are roughly additive.A one standard deviation increase in both conﬁdence measures would result in about 38 fewer burglaries per 100,000 population, an estimated9.8%dropfromthemeanburglary rate (((-18.428 + -19.647) / 389) ¥ 100).

- 15. Changes in crime rates have also

been linked with abortion policies and the accompanying implications for fertility. As explained by Levitt (2004: 181–182), the basic argument is based on the following premises: ‘1) unwanted children are at greater risk for crime, and 2) legalized abortion leads to a reduction in the number of unwanted births.’ The abortion thesis has been supported by several studies (see Levitt 2004: 183), although Zimring (2006a: 221) cautions that the evidence for an impact in Europe is at best ambiguous.

- 16. Historical research on crime trends


has been more likely to incorporate a crossnational perspective than the analyses of the recent crime drop. For example, in his study of murder in the USA. from historical times up to the present, Lane (1997: 297) observes that the experience of rising crime in the USA in the 1960s‘. . . was part of an international shift of great signiﬁcance. . .’. We thank an anonymous reviewer for this reference. Examining a period reaching further back in time, Gurr (1989: 38) concludes that European and US trends in homicide ‘parallel’ one another between the 1930s and the late 1980s. See also Eisner (2008: 310) for evidence of similarities between the USA and European nations in the‘peaking’ of the homicide rate in the early 1990s.
