# Age-Cohort Analysis of Arrest Rates

#### David F. Greenberg' and Nancy J. Larkin'

Logistic regressions of age-period-cohort models for city arrest rates are estimated with data from seven U.S. cities for the years 1970-1980 to study the dependence of officially designated criminality in select offense categories on age. A sensitivity analysis is carried out to determine how dependent the findings are on details of model specification.

KEY WORDS: crime; arrest rates; cohort; age; age-period-cohort models.

## 1. AGE AND CRIME: A REVIEW AND CRITIQUE OF THE EVIDENCE

The proposition that involvement in crime diminishes with age is one of the oldest and most widely accepted in criminology. Explanations remain the subject of controversy (Friday and Hage, 1976; Greenberg, 1977, 1983,

1985; Glaser, 1978; Cline, 1980; Hirschi and Gottfredson, 1983; Langan

and Farrington, 1983), but the proposition itself has gone unchallenged.

Two sorts of evidence are considered to have established this proposition empirically. The first consists of interviews or ethnographic observation of delinquents in the field (Matza, 1964; West, 1978, 1979). Researchers utilizing these methods commonly report that many of their subjects abandon delinquency as they reach the end of adolescence. However, these studies draw on small and possibly unrepresentative samples and have short follow-ups. Other studies, also based on small samples, have located groups in which criminality persists into adulthood, at least for some offense categories (Petersilia et al., 1978, pp. 14 and 27; Horowitz, 1983; McCord and Sanchez, 1983). It is not inconceivable that the reduction in criminal involvement in the former group is compensated by increased involvement in the latter, eliminating any relationship between age and crime in the aggregate population. Assuming the absence ofsystematic bias in arrest statistics, these figures seem to show that criminality does indeed decline with age. However, consideration must be given to the possible confounding of age, period, and cohort effects (Glenn, 1976, 1977). The term "cohort" refers to a group of individuals who experience the same significant life event (such as a birth) at about the same time. A cohort effect is an influence on a given cohort that persists as the cohort gets older. In contrast, a period effect is one that does not persist at later times but, rather, influences all cohorts at a given time. Thus, nonenduring effects of a war or depression are period effects, and enduring effects on those who were at a sensitive age during these events are cohort effects. The three sorts of effects can be seen in the hypothetical crime rates in Tables IIa, IIb, and IIc, which are constructed to represent pure cohort, period, and age effects, respectively.

It can be equally difficult to distinguish age from cohort effects when examining data collected at a single time. In Table IIa, each hypothetical cohort begins committing crimes at age 15 and continues to do so at a rate that remains constant as the cohort gets older. However, the younger cohorts (those in the lower left-hand part of the table) do so at higher rates than the older cohorts. In any given year, then, older cohorts commit crimes at

In recent decades, arrest rates have been rising, not falling; consequently it seems unlikely that longitudinal studies of single cohorts have confused period with age effects. However, the possibility that all or part of the age effects that appear in arrest data for a single year are really cohort effects needs to be considered. Indeed, in a study of the arrest histories of criminal offenders in Washington, D.C., Blumstein and Cohen (1979) discovered that once cohort was controlled, arrests did not decline with age. It is important in relation to current theoretical controversies about the meaning of the age patterns seen in cross-sectional data (Hirschi and Gottfredson, 1983; Greenberg, 1985) to determine whether this is so in other populations.

The question of whether age effects are real is also important in connection with policy questions. Boland and Wilson (1978) have argued for changes in the way youthful offenders are handled, on the grounds that there is a strong age-crime relationship. Opponents of new prison construction commonly argue that as the baby-boom cohort gets older, the crime and arrest rate will decline, reducing the caseload that criminal-justice agencies will have to process. This argument, too, rests on the assumption that there is a genuine nonspurious relationship between age and crime.

Here we address the empirical question of whether age-related patterns in cross-sectional data or in longitudinal studies of single cohorts might actually reflect period or cohort effects (or both), through a multivariate analysis that seeks to distinguish age, period, and cohort effects. Our focus is on aggregate rates, thereby precluding the possible identification of

subgroups whose age pattern deviates from the norm. However, the

methodological problems that arise in studying age, period, and cohort effects can be addressed with aggregate data. Moreover, the aggregate pattern is of interest in its own right, e.g., in connection with efforts to project future criminal-justice caseloads.

## 2. DATA AND METHODS

Our analysis is based on the frequencies of arrests in different crime categories for the years 1970, 1975, and 1980 for the following seven cities: Atlanta, Chicago, Denver, Knoxville, San Jose, Spokane, and Tucson. These cities were chosen from the 25 largest cities for which the FBI was willing to provide unpublished arrest statistics and were selected to ensure geographical representativeness within the limits imposed by financial constraints on the coding and punching ofdata. The present analysis is restricted to the arrest patterns for all cities aggregated. However, Chicago was

of violence crimes and total index crimes because anomalies in the aggravated-assault arrest figures suggest that sometime during the 1970-1980 decade, Chicago changed its recording procedures for assault arrests.

## The identification issue for age-cohort analysis has been discussed

extensively in recent literature (Glenn, 1976, 1977; Pullum, 1977; Fienberg and Mason, 1978; Palmore, 1978). It has become clear that the most general age-period-cohort model cannot be identified on the basis of statistical considerations alone because there is an exact linear relationship among age, period, and cohort effects. Although underidentification of the most general model precludes a purely empirical identification, it does not rule out an identification that makes some use of a priori knowledge about the parameters (Smith et al., 1982). It can be shown that a single linear constraint among the parameters of the model suffices to just-identify it. This approach has been used by Mason et al. (1973) and Knope and Hout (1974) among others. However, this approach must be treated with caution, for the assumption that a particular pair of parameters is equal may be dubious on substantive grounds, and estimates of the remaining parameters sensitive to the assumption. We have determined in our own data that if two of the older-age effects are constrained to be equal, parameter estimates for age effects in the younger years are sensitive to the precise choice of effects so constrained.

Pullum (1977) adopts an alternative procedure. First, he estimates all the two-factor solutions (age-period, age-cohort, period-cohort). Each of these solutions is overidentified. Then he constrains the ratio of two cohort effects in the three-factor model in such a manner as to minimize deviations from a weighted sum of the two-factor models. However, this is an arbitrary procedure. Although it will correctly recover a true two-factor model, its performance with three-factor models remains to be determined.

We handle the identification problem by estimating all one- and twofactor models and certain three-factor models for each offense. Because we are interested in age effects, we do not impose any constraints on the age parameters. Cross-sectional age distributions of criminality decline in the advanced as well as the earlier years, and it seems best not to rule out in advance the possibility that these declines represent direct age effects. We also elect not to impose equality constraints on the cohort effects. Elders often complain that the younger generation lacks respect for law and order.

233

To constrain a pair of cohorts to be equally crime-prone is to deny for at least one pair of cohorts that this is so. But we know of no substantive grounds for such a denial.

The lack of major social upheavals in the 1970-1980 decade suggests that the assumption that period effects were equal may not be far off the mark. We thus estimate models in which the effect of 1970 is assumed to be equal to that of 1975 and models in which the effect of 1975 is taken to be equal to that of 1980. Parameter estimates for these models are generally consistent with one another, and the free period parameters generally close

to those constrained.

We report here only estimates from the models in which the 1975 parameter is constrained to be equal to the 1980 parameter. Since a war was being fought in 1970 but not in 1975 or 1980, we thought this assumption more plausible than the assumption that period effects for 1970 and 1975 were equal. Of course other factors, such as changing levels of unemployment, could have given rise to year effects, making our assumption somewhat uncertain. For this reason we carry out a sensitivity analysis to determine the effect of varying the assumptions about period effects on our estimates.

Where the three-factor model is a significantly better fit at the 0.05 level than all the two-factor models, the three-factor model is adopted. However, when the two-factor models give fits that are not statistically worse than that of the three-factor model, they are adopted. In several instances we obtain good fits for more than one two-factor model. In each case where that happens, though, the age effects prove to be similar. In no case does a one-factor model provide a statistically acceptable fit.

The age dependence of the age parameters is quite different for the different offense categories. Property-crime arrests decline steadily from a maximum in the age 15-19 quinquennium; drunken driving rises to age 35-39 (perhaps reflecting increased time behind the wheel with age) and then declines. Violent crime shows a similar pattern but declines with age more slowly than drunken driving. The dip in total offense age parameters

The models for the individual violence and property crimes also show a high variability in the dependence of age parameters on age (Figs. 2 and 3). While the age parameters for murder and rape decline steadily with age from peaks at age 15-19, those for aggravated assault rise to age 35-39. Auto theft, burglary, and robbery all show similar patterns to rape and murder, but larceny declines to age 20-24 and is then virtually flat out to

age 55-59.

Taken at face value, these findings are striking. Offenses that load on the same factors in cross-sectional factor analyses of crime rates show extremely different age dependences. Whatever social, psychological, or

biological factors contribute to age dependence seem to vary strongly from one offense to another.

There are, however, reasons for thinking that our findings should not be taken at face value. All our estimates for {APC} models are derived on the assumption that two period effects are exactly equal. This assumption is not testable, because these models are just-identified. To determine the sensitivity of our results to this assumption, we estimated models in which the period dependence was assumed to be linear or quadratic or to follow a 3/2 power law. Some of these models showed age effects similar to those reported here. For example, in all specifications, auto theft declines steadily from its peak age of 15-19. Drunken driving rose and then fell in all models, though in some models the peaks occurred earlier (age 25-29) and in others later (age 35-39). On the other hand, the patterns for many offenses proved

to be highly model dependent. For example, in some models the age

parameters for robbery declined with age, but in other models they rose with age, and in still other models they increased to a peak at age 30-34 and then declined slowly. Rodgers (1982) has also found parameter estimates in age-cohort analyses to be sensitive to model specification.

Little confidence can be placed in findings that are so sensitive to model specification. In that circumstance, the investigator may be forced to specify theoretically what it is about a period or cohort that is relevant and then introduce these variables explicitly into the model. Doing so eliminates the identification problems encountered here, but its feasibility is, of course, contingent

on the investigator's ability to conceptualize and measure the relevant variables. In this respect, the situation is not much different from that encountered in most multivariate statistical analyses.

Replication can be another potentially useful strategy. Consider those models in the present study that could not be plausibly identified because age effects proved to be too sensitive to model specification. Our belief that age effects of some sort exist is not seriously shaken by this finding, because an association between age and crime has been reported in many different countries and periods over the last 150 years. We can dismiss the possibility that these associations are entirely the product of period and cohort effects because these should vary with time and place. Consistency across social conditions tells us that there are some age effects that are common to a wide range of social conditions? In arriving at this conclusion we use our general understanding of period and cohort effects to supplement purely statistical reasoning.
