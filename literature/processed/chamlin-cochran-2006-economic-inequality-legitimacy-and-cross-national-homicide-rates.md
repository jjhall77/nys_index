This research is concerned with explicating and modeling the causal linkages from economic inequality to homicide among nation-states. Specifically, the authors posit that the effect of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates is mediated by the perceived legitimacy of the system of stratification; that is, the effect of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates should be substantially attenuated once perceived legitimacy is controlled. The authors test this hypothesis with data from 33 of the 44 nationstates that participated in the third wave of the World Values Survey. Negative binomial regression analyses reveal that perceptions of legitimacy do not mediate the effects of economic inequality on homicide. However, they do indicate that the impact of economic, and political, illegitimacy on homicide depends on the level of modernity. The implications of these findings for the economic inequality–homicide relationship are explored.

Keywords: inequality; homicide; legitimacy; cross-national

For many researchers, the connection between the unequal distribution of economic rewards and homicide is so intuitively obvious that it requires little, if any, clarification. Independent of the difficulties inherent in making inferences about the behavior of individuals on the basis of macro-level findings (Alker, 1969; Robinson, 1950), the social–psychological frustration–aggression thesis is just one of several abstract explanations that can account for the economic inequality–homicide relationship. Thus, until we can derive and measure the variables that link economic inequality to cross-national differences in homicide rates, neither the frustration–aggression thesis, nor any competing hypothesis, can be falsified. Consequently, the goal of the current investigation is to discern, and explicitly include in our model specifications, indicators of the macro-social constructs that can account for the effects of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates.

We begin this endeavor with a review and synthesis of the various explanations that have been invoked to account for the positive association between economic inequality and homicide. Based on the insights garnered from a systematic review of this literature, we next specify, and confront with data, competing models that incorporate measures of the intervening causal processes that link indicators of economic inequality to cross-national homicide rates.

### The Economic Inequality–Homicide Linkage

Based on the empirical relationship between economic inequality and homicide among nation-states, one might be tempted to infer that economic inequality is inherently criminogenic. Such, however, may not be the case. Rather, as the structuralfunctionalist perspective has long recognized, the distribution of social and economic rewards can actually promote, rather than disrupt, macro-social integration.

According to functionalist theory (Durkheim, 1933, 1951; Parsons, 1977), small societies are composed of extended kinship groups that tend to exhibit minimal differentiation in terms of the symbolic (e.g., language, religion, values) and organizational (i.e., social and economic stratification) dimensions of social life. However, as social systems experience growth in population size and density, they are forced to

develop more efficient means of producing and distributing goods and services to their members (Parsons, 1977, pp. 38-50). For Durkheim (1933, pp. 7-110), the solution to this dilemma is the transformation of society from one that is relatively homogenous and undifferentiated to one that is progressively more heterogenous and differentiated (i.e., an elaboration of the division of labor).

An unavoidable, but not necessarily pernicious, outcome of the progressive division of labor is the production of social and economic inequalities. Inexorably, social systems must find ways of enticing individuals with the requisite aptitude and/or training to accept positions in the social structure that serve the greater good of society and their own particularistic interests (i.e., solve the problem of social stratification). From a functionalist perspective, the solution to the problem of how to best achieve an optimal division of labor is rather straightforward. If society places a premium on particular roles and duties, then it must provide sufficient pecuniary and social rewards to encourage the best and the brightest to compete for those positions in the social structure (Durkheim, 1933; Parsons, 1951, 1977; Spencer, 1972). This means, of course, that some members of society will inevitably accrue more wealth, prestige, and respect than others. As Davis and Moore (1945) observed more than half a century ago, “Social inequality is thus an unconsciously evolved device by which societies insure that the most important positions are conscientiously filled by the most qualified persons” (p. 243).

In sum, social and economic inequalities, insofar as they emerge from competition and merit, contribute to the healthy elaboration of the division of labor and, thereby, to macro-social integration (Durkheim, 1933; Parsons, 1977, pp. 208-209). However, social and economic inequalities that arise from invidious distinctions, that have little, if anything, to do with innate ability or industriousness, are quite another matter.

#### Ascribed Inequalities and Homicide

If, as functionalist theory suggests, social and economic inequalities promote macro-social integration, then how can they also engender homicide? For a number of macro-sociologists, the resolution to this paradox rests with the consolidation, over time, between achieved and ascribed positions in the social structure.

To begin, Messner (1982, pp. 228-229) suggests that according to Durkheim (1933), the normal development of the division of labor depends on the allocation of societal roles and positions according to individual talents and abilities. Moreover, based on insights garnered from Giddens (1971a, 1971b, 1976), he further asserts that the normal development of the division of labor contributes to macro-social integration in modern, complex societies by instilling feelings of mutual respect, concern, and cooperation among the citizenry (what he refers to as “moral individualism”). Thus, according to Messner (p. 229), the normal division of labor encourages prosocial behavior by replacing kinship and other informal ties that promote conformity in undifferentiated social systems with new norms and values associated with the rise of moral individualism.

If the continual division of labor provides a new and sustaining source of social solidarity, then how can it simultaneously promote higher rates of homicide? Drawing on the analysis of Goldthorpe (1974), Messner (1982) argues that achieved inequalities are translated (especially intergenerationally) into unfair advantage in ensuing competitions for social rewards and statuses. Thus, what ostensibly originates from the normal development of the division of labor, the pairing of individual talents with commensurate social responsibilities, is hypothesized to transmute into what Durkheim (1933, p. 375) identified as the second pathological form of the division of labor—the forced division of labor, where the tasks assigned to individuals do not match their natural talents. To summarize, Messner (pp. 229-330) theorizes that achieved economic inequalities often become reified into ascribed social and economic inequalities. As a result, they weaken the moral restraining influence of the social order and, thereby, lead to higher levels of homicide.

Judith Blau and Peter Blau (1982), albeit for different reasons, also contend that ascribed inequalities are criminogenic. Instead of focusing on the influence of ascribed inequalities on the regulation of passions and the internalization of norms and values (i.e., anomie), they are more concerned with the impact that ascribed inequalities have on the attitudes and behaviors of those mired in the lower economic strata.

Drawing on the work of a variety of social theorists (Blau, 1977; Coser, 1956; Simmel, 1950), Blau and Blau (1982) contend that pronounced economic inequalities, particularly when they coexist within an ideological climate that embraces rational–legal authority and egalitarian values, tend to be viewed as unjust. Furthermore, the more that economic inequalities are correlated with ascribed characteristics (e.g., race, religion, or ethnicity), the more difficult it becomes for the disadvantaged to escape their poverty through individual initiative or collective action (Blau, 1964; Simmel, 1950). Thus, Blau and Blau conclude that “great economic inequalities generally foster conflict and violence, but ascriptive inequalities do so particularly” (p. 119).

#### Relative Deprivation and Homicide

Blau and Blau (1982) may have been among the first to appreciate the role that ascribed economic inequalities play in the production of feelings of relative deprivation among the poor (Messner, 1989). However, the observation that relative deprivation, regardless of its convergence with other social statuses, generates feelings of anger and frustration among the less fortunate is derived from two long-enduring sociological traditions. The first, Mertonian anomie theory, implicates the contradictions between the economic structure and cultural values of market economies (Merton, 1938). The other, rooted in Marxist theory, implicates the contradictions that emerge from the inexorable progression of the capitalist mode of production (Engels, 1973; Lenin, 1939; Lynch, 1987).

Merton (1938, 1957) argues that the social structure cannot effectively deliver what the dominant value system promises: universal access to legitimate means to secure material success goals. According to Merton, American culture places a preeminent

emphasis on monetary rewards (i.e., the so-called American Dream). Although socially accepted methods for acquiring property are also instilled in the populace, a preoccupation with the ends often relegates the means to a position of lesser importance. When the social structure fails to provide sufficient means to achieve success goals in the prescribed fashion, an increase in the rate of crime is the anticipated result as societal members begin to employ “innovative,” but illegitimate, means to acquire the various symbols of material success. Hence, in the context of blocked opportunities, the contradictions between the values concerning the means and ends produce a state of anomie that, in turn, frees some segments of society to engage in criminal behavior, especially those forms that allow them to procure financial rewards.

The extension of Mertonian anomie theory to the cross-national association between economic inequality and homicide rates is relatively straightforward. The more that economic inequality emerges from blocked opportunities, rather than merit, the higher the probability it will produce feelings of anger and frustration in those individuals forced to accept less financially remunerative positions in the labor market. Thus, from this perspective, economic inequality engenders feelings of relative deprivation and higher rates of homicide because it is, for some portion of the population, undeserved (Hansmann & Quigley, 1982; Krahn et al., 1986; Lee, 2001; Stack, 1984).

Marxist theory also views relative economic deprivation as undeserved by those who experience it. In contrast to Merton’s (1938, 1957) approach, cultural factors play no role in the genesis of economic inequality. Rather, it is the indefatigable need of capitalists to acquire more and more capital for modernization and expansion and the consequent exploitation of workers to pay for these dual exigencies of capitalism that lead to ever-increasing levels of economic inequality (Lynch, 1987).

Although Marx (1906) is primarily concerned with the teleological evolution of the capitalist mode of production, Marxist criminology is not. Criminologists working within the Marxist tradition focus on the immediate effects of capitalist exploitation on the behavior of workers. For example, Bonger (1916, pp. 401-407) theorizes that capitalism promotes selfishness, hedonism, and egoism among workers. As a result of these “dehumanizing” influences, the proletariat become more willing to act without considering the consequences of their actions for others. Accordingly, criminality is viewed as one of the more efficacious means of securing basic needs and desires for the working class—a rational response to capitalist exploitation and deprivation.

Lynch, Groves, and Lizotte (1994), extrapolating from Marx’s (1906, 1963) discussion of the relationship between the maturation of capitalism and the expropriation of surplus labor value, provide further insight into the connection between economic inequality and crime. Specifically, Lynch et al. contend that as capitalist societies become more advanced, the exploitation of workers increases at an increasing rate (to finance modernization). Modernization, in turn, produces a reduction in the demand for labor, thereby increasing the supply of surplus labor (see also Spitzer, 1975). Consequently, “a larger portion of the population is placed in structural locations conducive to crime” (Lynch et al., 1994, p. 16).

#### Synthesis: Economic Inequality, Legitimacy, and Homicide

Why is economic inequality so detrimental to social relations within a community? As we have seen, it all depends on how it is produced. Each of the theoretical arguments that we have reviewed to this point claims that economic inequality is criminogenic because the social processes that generate it are intrinsically unfair. There may be some controversy as to the specific manner in which societies “unjustly” distribute pecuniary resources to their members. Indeed, whether or not it emerges from the progressive accumulation of competitive advantage (Messner, 1982), the correlation between ascribed and achieved social statuses (Blau & Blau, 1982), the capitalist mode of production (Marx, 1906), or the juxtaposition of blocked opportunities with an overemphasis on success goals (Merton, 1938) are the subjects of considerable debate. Nonetheless, there is substantial consensus among macro-social criminologists, working within both the functionalist and conflict traditions, that accidents of birth (ascribed inequalities), rather than merit (achieved inequalities), are primarily responsible for the unequal distribution of income both within and across modern societies.

Consequently, we posit that ascribed, rather than achieved, inequalities engender a culture of deprivation, frustration, and anger among societal members. Social and economic inequalities that result from open and fair competitive processes are probably viewed by most individuals as legitimate. Social and economic inequalities that are a consequence of unfair advantage and/or invidious distinctions are probably viewed by most individuals as illegitimate.

### The Present Investigation

Based on a review of the cross-national literature, we have deduced that social and economic inequalities that are based on factors other than merit undermine the legitimacy of the social structure. Furthermore, as the social system loses its legitimacy, it simultaneously loses its moral authority and, thereby, its capacity to regulate the behavior of societal members (Messner, 1988). As ties to the larger social order weaken, individuals become free to pursue their selfish wants and desires. This line of reasoning suggests to us two possible mechanisms that can provide the linkages between economic inequality and cross-national homicide.

To begin, we predict that the effect of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates is mediated by the perceived legitimacy of a society’s system of the stratification. To recap, our basic position is that ascribed economic inequalities, rather than economic inequality per se, generate macro-level variations in homicide rates. If such is the case, then the positive association between generic-measures economic inequality and cross-national homicide rates found in the empirical literature should be attenuated by perceptions of the legitimacy of the social and economic order. Insofar as societal members view the system of economic stratification as inequitable, explicitly

controlling for a measure of perceptions of legitimacy should substantially reduce the effects of the overall level of economic inequality on homicide.

Based on the recent theoretical work of Chamlin and Cochran (2005), we also explore the possibility that the effect of perceived legitimacy on cross-national homicide rates might be conditioned by levels of modernity. To explicate, Chamlin and Cochran posit that the interrelationships among economic inequality, the legitimacy of social institutions, and cross-national homicide rates may depend on the level of societal development. Drawing on the observations of an eclectic array of social theorists (Blau & Blau, 1982; Blau, 1964; Bonger, 1916; Bukovansky, 2002; Ford, 1953; Lenin, 1939; Steinberg, 1978; Weber, 1968), they conclude that an important consequence of the evolution of societies from traditional, agrarian communities to industrialized, modern social systems is the increasing rejection of custom and heredity to legitimate the extant social, economic, and political order. Rather, modern societies, particularly those grounded in the European intellectual revolutions of the 18th and 19th centuries (e.g., rationalism and romanticism) tend to rely on rational–legal authority to legitimate the manner in which they allocate positions in the social structure (Durkheim, 1933, 1938; Lefebvre, 1967; Locke, 1982; Marx, 1906, 1963; Voltaire, 1971; Weber, 1968).

This line of reasoning leads us to predict that the perceived legitimacy of the stratification system might be expected to mediate the effects of economic inequality on homicide in modern societies but have little, if any, effect in less developed ones. Therefore, we also assess the joint influence of perceived legitimacy and modernity on homicide for our sample of nation-states.

### Procedure

#### Sample

The initial sample for this investigation consists of the 44 nation-states that participated in the third wave of the World Values Survey (WVS; 1995-1997), our data source for the measures of legitimacy (World Values Study Group, 1995-1997). Sample attrition, primarily for the homicide and economic inequality measures, reduces the final sample size to 33.

The WVS, which grew out of a study conducted by the 1981 European Values Survey group, is designed to provide cross-national data amenable to the examination of the influence of the cultural dimensions of social life on political and social systems. Specifically, the WVS utilizes stratified, multistage, probability sampling techniques to measure cross-cultural attitudes, values, and beliefs of adult citizens, 18 and older. Thus, we decided to examine the survey questions incorporated with each of the three available waves of the WVS that we might be able to discern a credible measure of legitimacy.

As it turned out, the third wave (1995-1997) of the WVS contained a number of items that looked promising. In brief, the third wave of the WVS was administered to more than 60 macro-social units (both nation-states and subnational regions), representing a majority of the world’s population, various levels of economic prosperity (with per capita incomes as low as $300 a year to societies with per capita incomes as high as $30,000 per year), and various forms of political representation (from long-established democracies to authoritarian regimes).

#### The Dependent Variable

Initially, the dependent variable is operationalized as the rate of homicides known to the police per 100,000 persons. The primary source for this measure is the sixth United Nations Survey on Crime Trends and Operations of the Criminal Justice Systems (UNCJS; United Nations Office on Drugs and Crime, 2002). Whenever possible, we replace missing data from the UNCJS with official data from Interpol (2000) and the United Nations Economic Commission for Europe (2003).

We recognize that there is some debate concerning what is the best data source for the measurement of homicide across nation-states. Some prefer using World Health Organization data (Avison & Loring, 1986; Messner et al., 2002; Savolainen, 2000). Others, including ourselves, choose to employ data collected by crime control agencies (Krahn et al., 1986; Messner, 1982; Nalla & Newman, 1994). Regardless, two considerations impel us to use the UN crime survey data in lieu of the World Health Organization vital statistics. First, the former data source, which like Interpol reports official counts of crime, has been found to be as, if not more, reliable than its competitors (Bennett & Lynch, 1990; Newman, 1999). Second, this resource provides more complete homicide data for a greater number of nation-states than do the alternative available sources.1

To minimize yearly fluctuations in reporting, crime figures were averaged, when possible, for the 3 years after the administration of the third wave of the WVS (1998-2000).

#### Legitimacy

To reiterate, the present investigation focuses on the extent to which the legitimacy of the economic order can account for the cross-national relationship between economic inequality and homicide. However, as has been long recognized, an unfair system of economic stratification can readily undermine the legitimacy of the larger social and political order (Durkheim, 1933; Lefebvre, 1967; Marx, 1906; O’Connor, 1973). Hence, we decided, if possible, to attempt to locate indicators that tap the legitimacy of other social institutions and that of the economy.

Our perusal of the third wave of the WVS revealed two survey questions that, on their face, appeared to be valid indicators of two distinct, yet complementary, dimensions of legitimacy. The first question taps respondents’beliefs concerning the fairness

of the system of economic stratification (economic illegitimacy). Specifically, we measure perceived economic illegitimacy as the percentage of respondents who state that people in their country are poor because society treats them unfairly. The second question taps respondents’ beliefs about the legitimacy of the national government (political illegitimacy). We measure perceived political illegitimacy as the percentage of respondents who believe that the government is run to further the interests of social elites rather than for the benefit of all.

#### Modernity

Most cross-national researchers, working in the Durkheimian tradition, focus on the interrelationships among the inexorable elaboration of the division of labor, the rise of individualism, and social deviance. According to this view, the progressive differentiation of the social structure lessens the capacity of the normative order to control the behavior of societal members, even within social systems that have made the initial transition from mechanical to organic forms of solidarity. As a consequence, individuals are freed from the constraining influence of traditional authority to pursue selfish, particularistic interests, including engaging in deviant and/or criminal behaviors. Thus, the modernization process, in and of itself, is expected to promote crime and deviance (Krohn, 1976, 1978; Messner, 1982; Webb, 1972).

Following past research (Krohn, 1978; Messner, 1982), we tap the concept of modernization with five measures of structural, demographic, and technological change. These include the percentage of the population that is urban (1997), the percentage of the population 15 to 24 (1995), the number of telephone main lines per 1,000 population (1997), the per capita electric power consumption (1997), and the gross domestic product per capita (1997). In brief, energy consumption and advances in communication are integral to the process of modernization, whereas increasing urbanization, a growth in the gross domestic product per capita, and a declining birth rate are typical consequences of it. Data for each of these variables were ascertained from the World Development Indicators Online (World Bank, 2002).

The inclusion of a number of indicators of societal development in the model specifications may serve to minimize the risk of omitted variable bias. Unfortunately, it simultaneously increases the probability that the final equations may be affected by multicollinearity, thereby producing unstable parameter estimates (especially in situations, such as ours, where the ratio of variables to degrees of freedom is small). One solution to this dilemma that has emerged in the literature is the use of principal components analysis to reduce the number of predictors prior to the estimation of any prediction equations (Land, McCall, & Cohen, 1990; Morenoff & Sampson, 1997). This is the approach that we take here.

#### Economic Inequality

As has been acknowledged elsewhere (Messner & Rosenfeld, 1997; Stack, 1984), it is virtually impossible to locate cross-national measures of economic inequality for a single year. Consequently, our estimate of relative economic deprivation, the Gini index of economic concentration, spans a number of years, circa 1997. This measure was obtained from the World Development Indicators (World Bank, 2001).

#### Population Size

Population size is included to control for the predictions derived from formal structural theory (Blau, 1977; Gibbs & Erickson, 1976). The causal linkages are straightforward. Increases in population size promote various social interactions, including homicide, by reducing the physical distances among members of a community while simultaneously increasing the number of potential associates within a community. Interestingly, because the opportunity assumption is silent with respect to the content of structurally induced social contacts, this variant of structural theory anticipates finding a positive correlation between population size and integrative (e.g., marriage), and conflictive (e.g., criminality), social interactions (Blau, 1977, p. 163; Mayhew & Levinger, 1976, p. 94). Population size (1997), which is measured as the number of inhabitants within the country in units of 1,000, was obtained from the World Development Indicators Online (World Bank, 2002).

#### Sex Ratio

Messner and Sampson (1991) suggest that the sex ratio exerts two countervailing influences on the rate of homicide. According to their reasoning, a high ratio of males

to females means that there are more people (males) in the population who are at high risk for being involved in homicides. Statistically, this is a compositional effect. Alternatively, a high ratio of males to females is expected to reduce levels of family disruption, which, in turn, is expected to reduce the level of homicide. The overall effect of the sex ratio depends on the strength of these countervailing processes.

Consistent with the family disruption thesis, a number of cross-national studies report that the partial effect of the sex ratio on homicide is negative (Messner et al., 2002; Messner & Rosenfeld, 1997; Pratt & Godsey, 2003). Hence, we include the sex ratio in 1995 (males per 100 females) as a statistical control. The source for this measure is the World Population Prospects: The 2004 Revision (United Nations, 2005).

#### Interaction Effects

As we explained above, we also decided to explore the possibility that the effects of perceived legitimacy on homicide may depend on levels of modernity. To evaluate these interaction effects, we constructed two product terms (Political Illegitimacy × Modernity and Economic Illegitimacy × Modernity) and examined the extent to which their inclusion improved on the fit of the direct effects models (Allison, 1977).

### Results

#### Preliminary Data Analyses

Before presenting the findings from our final models, we consider the statistical adequacy of the initial ordinary least squares (OLS) regression solutions. Even after one successfully simplifies the regressor space by the application of principal component analytic techniques (as we did above), the multivariate analysis of macro-level data from small samples (i.e., 33 nation-states) can produce heteroskedastic error structures and/or pronounced collinearity. Therefore, we performed a number of diagnostic tests to determine whether or not the preliminary solutions violate the basic assumptions that underlie OLS regression analysis.

Although high zero-order correlations between predictor variables do not, in and of themselves, indicate that multicollinearity is present (Hanushek & Jackson, 1977, p. 90), the simple correlations between economic inequality and the sex ratio (–.62) is large enough to warrant concerns about this issue.2 To assess the extent to which the strong bivariate association between economic inequality and the sex ratio influences the ensuing multivariate analyses, we first reestimated each of the equations, removing each of the two potentially collinear predictors, one at a time. These modifications to the model specifications produce minute fluctuations in the standard errors and no noticeable changes in the findings, suggesting that the correlation between economic inequality and the sex ratio is not tainting the multivariate analyses (Maddala, 2001). Second, we examined the variation inflation factor (VIF) scores associated with the

initial OLS regression equations. None of the VIF scores, for any of the additive models, exceeds 3. Because a VIF value in excess of 10 is generally considered evidence of multicollinearity (Neter, Wasserman, & Kutner, 1990), we conclude that the VIF scores, along with the results from backwards deletion procedure, indicate that multicollinearity is not a problem.

We also explored the extant possibility that the error terms are heteroskedastic. We calculated the Cook-Weisberg test statistics to evaluate the null hypothesis that the model residuals from each of the OLS regression models equations are homoskedastic. Unfortunately, the magnitudes of the test statistics are quite large, ranging from a low of 32.6 (p < .0001) to a high of 39.1 (p < .0001), and thereby indicate that error terms are heteroskedastic.

#### Heteroskedasticity in Cross-National Setting

Hannon and Knapp (2003) contend, in a recent critique of the common practice of transforming a dependent variable by its natural logarithm to induce homoskedastic model residuals, that researchers often select one means of correcting for unequal error variances in lieu of another without fully considering how their decision can affect the functional form of the subsequent analyses. Although we are not in full agreement with all of their conclusions concerning the relative merits of alternative procedures for dealing with heteroskedasticity, we concur with their basic point that one should assess the appropriateness of a given remedy for this problem before deciding to employ it.

Initially, we considered estimating weighted least squares (WLS) equations to increase the efficiency of the parameter estimates. This procedure requires one to determine, a priori, what is generating the heteroskedastic disturbance terms. Typically in macro-level research, the population size of the unit of analysis is responsible for the problem. However, further examination of the OLS model residuals, and subsequent data analyses, indicate that the unequal error variances do not emerge as a result of measurement error associated with the population size of the nation-states. Hence, we concluded that WLS regression models would not be helpful.

We also considered employing White’s (1980) correction of the standard error from the OLS regression solutions as a remedy. This strategy has the advantage of not requiring the researcher to make any assumptions about the underlying processes that might be producing the heteroskedastic errors. Unfortunately, Monte Carlo simulations conducted by Cribari-Neto and Zarkos (2001) reveal that White’s estimator is appreciably biased when the sample is small (the present situation).

Ultimately, we decided to employ Poisson regression techniques to model crossnational variations in homicide rates. As Osgood (2000) demonstrates, Poisson regression is well suited to the task of modeling rate measures when a significantly skewed dependent variable produces heteroskedastic error terms.

Two issues relating to the execution of the present investigation need to be discussed at this juncture. First, Poisson-based regression models are typically used to predict the

number of times an event occurs within a given time interval. Given that we are interested in predicting variations in the homicide rate, we must make a relatively simple adjustment to the standard model specification. One can effectively transform a count to a rate model by adding the natural logarithm of the deflator (i.e., population size) to the equation and giving that variable a fixed coefficient of one. Second, Poisson regression models presume that the model explains all the meaningful variation in the dependent measure. Because there is no a priori reason to assume that cross-national differences in homicide rates can be completely accounted for by the conventional Poisson regression model, it is considered prudent to also calculate the negative binomial variant of this regression model. The specification of a negative binomial Poisson regression model entails introducing an additional term (α) into the equation to capture the residual systematic variance in the underlying homicide rate (for a more sophisticated and detailed discussion of the Poisson distribution and Poisson regression techniques, see Gardner, Mulvey, & Shaw, 1995; Land, McCall, & Nagin, 1995; Osgood, 2000).

#### Conditional Effects Models

Table 3 contains the results from the negative binomial regression analyses of the conditional effects of perceptions of illegitimacy on cross-national homicides. Equation 4 (column 1) includes a product term to capture the joint effect of perceived economic illegitimacy and modernity on homicide, whereas Equation 5 (column 2) includes a product term to capture the joint effect of perceived political illegitimacy and modernity on homicide.

For each equation, we present the same information that is contained within Table 2. Thus, for each predictor, row 1 reports the metric coefficient, row 2 reports the standard error, row 3 reports the z score, and row 4 reports the incidence rate ratios associated with each of the parameter estimates.

The conditional effects of perceived illegitimacy on homicide are intriguing. Consistent with the direct effects models, controlling for the joint effects of perceived illegitimacy and modernity has no impact on the economic inequality–homicide relationship. In each of the multiplicative models, economic inequality continues to exert a significant, positive influence on the cross-national homicide rate (compare Equation 1 with Equations 4 and 5).

Although we find no evidence that the product terms mediate the effects of economic inequality on homicide, this does not mean that they do not affect the level of homicide. The negative binomial regression solutions clearly show that the impact of economic and political illegitimacy on homicide depends on levels of modernity. Though the magnitude of these effects is small (see the incident rate ratio for each of the product terms), they are in the expected direction. As we anticipated, based on the theoretical work of Chamlin and Cochran (2005), the sign of the coefficients for the Perceived Illegitimacy × Modernity interaction terms is positive, indicating that the criminogenic effects perceptions of economic and political illegitimacy are limited to modern societies.

Overall, the pattern of findings produced by negative binomial regression analyses is somewhat perplexing. As anticipated, we did find that perceptions of economic, and political, illegitimacy affect levels of homicide. These effects are conditional, depending on levels of societal development. Yet contrary to our expectations, we found no evidence that perceptions of either economic or political illegitimacy directly or conditionally mediate the effects of economic inequality on cross-national levels of homicide. ### Discussion

Numerous studies employing differing methodologies consistently have found a robust positive association between economic deprivation and cross-national homicide rates (Avison & Loring, 1986; Braithwaite, 1979; Braithwaite & Braithwaite, 1980; Hansmann & Quigley, 1982; Krahn et al., 1986; Krohn, 1976; Lee, 2001; Messner, 1982, 1989; Messner et al., 2002; Neapolitan, 1998; Pratt & Godsey, 2003). However, most of these researchers have failed to explicate, and none has specifically modeled, the macro-social mechanisms through which economic inequality is linked to homicide rates (Agnew, 1999; Chamlin & Cochran, 2005; Krahn et al., 1986; Lee, 2001). Informed by the contributions of a variety of functionalist, structuralist, and conflict or Marxist sociologists and criminologists (Blau & Blau, 1982; Blau, 1964, 1977; Bonger, 1916; Coser, 1956; Davis & Moore, 1945; Durkheim, 1933; Lynch, 1987; Lynch et al., 1994; Merton, 1938, 1957; Messner, 1982; Parsons, 1951, 1977; Simmel, 1950; Spencer, 1972; Spitzer, 1975), we make a case that ascribed economic inequalities (i.e., those based on unfair advantages, invidious distinctions, and/or accidents of birth), rather than achieved inequalities (i.e., those based on merit), are criminogenic because they are widely perceived as illegitimate (Chamlin & Cochran, 2005). As the social system loses its legitimacy, it also loses its capacity to regulate the behavior of its members (Messner, 1988). Thus, we posit that the legitimacy of the stratification process may well be the macro-social mechanism linking economic inequality to homicide.

Based on the work of a number of social theorists, we specify and estimate two alternative functional forms for this relationship. First, we explore the possibility that the effect of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates is mediated by the perceived legitimacy of the system of stratification; that is, the effect of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates should be substantially attenuated once perceived legitimacy is controlled. We tested this specification with data from 33 of the 44 nation-states that participated in the third wave of the WVS (World Values Study Group, 1995-1997) for which homicide and economic inequality measures could also be identified. The WVS provided us with two aggregate measures of the perceived legitimacy of the stratification system (economic and political illegitimacy). Our initial findings failed to support this specification. That is, neither measure of illegitimacy mediated the effects of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates.

Second, drawing on the work of Chamlin and Cochran (2005), we posit that the interrelationships among economic inequality, legitimacy, and cross-national homicide rates are conditioned by the level of societal development. That is, as societies evolve from traditional, agrarian communities to modern, industrialized societies, they increasingly reject custom and kinship as the legitimate basis for the social, economic, and political order and tend, instead, to rely on rational–legal authority to legitimate the manner in which positions in the social structure are allocated (Durkheim, 1933, 1938; Lefebvre, 1967; Locke, 1982; Marx, 1906, 1963; Voltaire, 1971; Weber, 1968).

If true, then it is possible that the mediating effects of legitimacy may be limited to modern societies. Accordingly, we tested an additional hypothesis, namely, that the extent to which perceived legitimacy mediates the effects of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates depends on levels of modernity. This hypothesis was not supported; controlling for the joint effects of economic and political illegitimacy and modernity had no impact on the economic inequality–homicide relationship.

Ironically, we did find that perceptions of illegitimacy, both economic and political dimensions, are associated with higher levels of homicide. Furthermore, these effects were found to vary across levels of societal development. Our measures of perceived illegitimacy positively affected homicide in modern nations but had no impact in less developed nations.

What are we to make of this seemingly paradoxical pattern of findings? Assuming, for the sake of this discussion, that our results do not reflect some methodological deficiency, then we need to reconsider the macro-social linkages from economic inequality to homicide. Clearly, these results indicate that perceptions of legitimacy do not mediate the effects of economic inequality on cross-national homicide rates. Of course, there may be other mechanisms through which economic inequality might influence levels of homicide. Indeed, we recommend that macro criminologists continue to attempt to identify and locate measures for alternative social processes that might explain the economic inequality–homicide relationship.

We should also acknowledge the possibility that the influence of economic inequality on homicide might have more to do with its deleterious effects on the physical, rather than the symbolic, aspects of social life. Independent of any of its social or psychological consequences, economic inequality can have a profound negative impact on the material conditions of existence. For example, increases in economic inequality have been reported to be associated with reduced educational attainment (Mayer, 2002), childhood malnutrition (Larrea & Kawachi, 2004), infant mortality rates (Mayer & Sarin, 2005), and general rates of morality (Thorbecke & Charumlind, 2002). Thus, regardless of social or psychic consequences of the unequal distribution of economic resources on a citizenry, its negative influence on the day-to-day aspects of living in society might be criminogenic (Currie, 1997).

Although we failed to find support for our hypotheses delineating the interrelationships among economic inequality, legitimacy, and homicide, this does not mean that our analyses were uninformative. Serendipitously, our effort to explicate the theoretical linkages from economic inequality to homicide led to the discovery of a conditional relationship between perceptions of economic and political legitimacy and homicide. Specifically, the multiplicative models revealed that the belief among societal members that the political, and the economic, order was illegitimate was associated with higher levels of homicide in more developed nations than in less developed ones.

We interpret these findings as supporting Chamlin and Cochran’s (2005, pp. 22-23) statements concerning the interrelationships among societal development, methods of legitimation, and homicide. Drawing on contributions of a number of theorists (e.g., Blau, 1964; O’Connor, 1973), they suggest that as nation-states modernize, they

tend to rely more on rational–legal (rather than charismatic or traditional) mechanisms to differentially distribute social rewards and statuses among societal members. As a result of this transition to a more egalitarian method of legitimizing the social structure, individuals become less willing to accept the exploitation of social institutions to promote the interests of one segment of the community in lieu of another. Thus, Chamlin and Cochran (p. 24), posit that within modern nation-states, the perception that the social system is illegitimate is particularly disintegrative. It weakens the moral authority of conventional norms and values and therefore promotes the incidence of selfish and hedonistic behavior, including homicide.

In sum, although the present investigation failed to empirically discern the linkages from economic inequality to cross-national homicide rates, it did reveal a substantively interesting conditional relationship between perceived illegitimacy and cross-national homicide rates. This is a potentially important finding that should generate further research into the manner in which perceptions of legitimacy, and other macro-cultural processes, affect cross-national crime rates.

Last, we wish to encourage macro criminologists to continue to attempt to discern the intervening factors that connect macro-structural conditions to criminal outcomes. All too often we successfully establish strong and stable statistical associations between macro-level predictors and crime rates, such as the economic inequality– homicide relationship, that are amenable to a variety of competing explanations. Only by attempting to identify and measure the intervening processes that might account for them can we convincingly falsify macro-criminological theory.
