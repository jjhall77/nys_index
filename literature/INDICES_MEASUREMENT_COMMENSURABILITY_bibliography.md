## Part 4 — Open Questions and Research Opportunities

### Opportunity 1: Bridge the CHI and Composite-Indicator Literatures
**Priority: HIGH**

**Gap:** No CHI paper cites the OECD Handbook, discusses compensability, or engages with Ravallion, Saltelli, or Sen. The CHI movement has built a consequential composite index without engaging the composite-indicator methodology literature.

**My data:** A fully operationalized CHI with block-level aggregation and multiple weight specifications.

**What I could do:** Explicitly frame the CHI as a composite indicator. Apply the OECD 10-step checklist. Discuss compensability. Reveal embedded trade-offs. Cite Sen's partial comparability as the philosophical basis for the enterprise. This would be a theoretical contribution that no CHI paper has made.

### Opportunity 2: Replicate the Blumstein Null at Block Level
**Priority: HIGH**

**Gap:** Blumstein (1974) showed inter-crime correlations above .958 at the national level, making weighting irrelevant. No study has replicated this test at the micro-geographic level for a U.S. city.

**My data:** NYPD complaint data for 10 offense categories across ~120K+ blocks.

**What I could do:** Compute inter-crime-type correlation matrices across blocks. If correlations are substantially lower than .958, this provides the direct empirical justification for block-level CHI: weighting matters precisely because crime types do *not* move together across micro-geographic units.

### Opportunity 3: Test Compensability at the Block Level
**Priority: MEDIUM**

**Gap:** Every CHI uses linear aggregation (full compensability). No study tests whether non-compensatory aggregation produces different targeting decisions.

**My data:** Block-level CHI under multiple specifications.

**What I could do:** Compare linear, geometric, and lexicographic aggregation rules for block ranking. If stable across rules, compensability is harmless in practice. If not, the aggregation function is a consequential design choice that the field has ignored.

### Opportunity 4: Reveal the Embedded Trade-Offs
**Priority: MEDIUM**

**Gap:** No CHI paper states its implied trade-off ratios. Users don't know that the index implies one murder = 3,650 bicycle thefts.

**My data:** Complete CHI weight table.

**What I could do:** Publish a "trade-off ratio table" showing what every weight implies in terms of equivalence to every other offense type. This transparency is mandated by the OECD framework and would be a first in the CHI literature.

### Opportunity 5: Dark-Figure Sensitivity Analysis
**Priority: MEDIUM**

**Gap:** No CHI adjusts for differential reportability across offense types.

**My data:** NYPD complaint data; NCVS-derived reporting rates by offense type.

**What I could do:** Construct a "reportability-adjusted" CHI that upweights low-reporting offenses (sexual assault, domestic violence) and test whether block-level rankings change. This would be the first CHI to engage with the Biderman-Reiss dark figure problem.

### Opportunity 6: Position the CHI in the Full Intellectual History
**Priority: LOWER (but high value for a review paper)**

**Gap:** The CHI literature traces its lineage to Sellin & Wolfgang (1964) and stops. The deeper roots — Sellin (1931), Wilkins (1963), Biderman & Reiss (1967), Blumstein (1974), Stevens (1946), the OECD composite-indicator tradition — are unacknowledged.

**My data:** This literature corpus.

**What I could do:** Write a literature review section (or standalone review paper) that traces the full lineage from 1831 to the present, connecting the CHI to the measurement science, social choice, and commensurability traditions. This would be a significant intellectual contribution that reframes the CHI as the latest chapter in a century-long effort.

### Opportunity 7: Engage with the Legal Scholarship on Overcriminalization
**Priority: MEDIUM-HIGH**

**Gap:** No CHI paper acknowledges that its weight source (statutory sentencing) comes from a politically pathological legislative process. Stuntz (2001) shows that criminal law expands via prosecutor-legislator collusion, producing overbroad statutes and enabling selective enforcement. Kleinfeld (2019) demonstrates that criminal statutes are instrumental law designed for plea bargaining, not textual constraint. Haugh (2015) argues that overcriminalization is itself criminogenic, reducing law legitimacy.

**My data:** A CHI built on NYS Penal Law statutory midpoints for 10 core target offenses.

**What I could do:** Be the first CHI paper to engage with the legal scholarship on overcriminalization. Argue that sentencing ranges for core offenses (murder, rape, robbery, assault, burglary, grand larceny) are insulated from the worst pathologies Stuntz and Kleinfeld describe: core-offense ranges are relatively stable, democratically enacted, and reflect genuine social judgments of harm severity. The CHI's use of sentencing ranges rather than charging decisions sidesteps the instrumental-text problem. Cite Haugh to argue that transparent, proportionate harm weighting could enhance rather than undermine perceived legitimacy.

## Part 5 — Recommended Research Priorities

### Priority 1: The CHI as Composite Indicator — Theoretical Framing
*Cross-references: Opportunities 1, 4, 6*

Frame the NYS CHI within the composite-indicator methodology tradition. Cite the OECD Handbook, Sen, Ravallion, Espeland & Stevens. Discuss compensability, reveal trade-off ratios, and use the robustness analysis as the composite-indicator-standard sensitivity test. This elevates the paper from a technical contribution to a theoretical one.

**Justification:** The CHI literature's failure to engage with the composite-indicator tradition is its biggest intellectual gap. Filling it positions your paper as the definitive methodological statement on what a CHI is and what it assumes.

### Priority 2: The Blumstein Test at the Block Level
*Cross-references: Opportunity 2*

Show empirically that inter-crime correlations across NYC blocks are low enough that weighting produces meaningfully different targeting decisions than counting. This provides the causal justification for the entire enterprise.

**Justification:** Blumstein's null result is the strongest objection to any CHI. Refuting it at the operational scale (blocks, not nations) with contemporary data closes the argument.

### Priority 3: Robustness and Compensability Testing
*Cross-references: Opportunities 2, 3*

Combine the existing robustness analysis (low/mid/high weights, different N thresholds) with compensability testing (linear vs. geometric vs. lexicographic aggregation). Report where in the ranking distribution specifications diverge, not just whether they do.

**Justification:** This directly addresses both Ravallion's "mashup index" critique and the practical question of whether targeting decisions are sensitive to CHI construction choices.

### Priority 4: Transparency — Trade-Off Ratios and Dark-Figure Acknowledgment
*Cross-references: Opportunities 4, 5*

Publish the embedded trade-off ratios. Acknowledge the dark-figure limitation. Optionally test reportability-adjusted weights.

**Justification:** The OECD framework demands transparency about what composites assume. No CHI has done this. First-mover advantage.

### Priority 5: The Full Intellectual History (Review Paper)
*Cross-references: Opportunity 6*

Write the review that connects Sellin (1931) → Wilkins (1963) → Biderman & Reiss (1967) → Blumstein (1974) → Sellin & Wolfgang (1964) / Rossi et al. (1974) → Parton et al. (1991) → Sherman et al. (2016) → the modern CHI movement. Integrate the measurement science, social choice, and commensurability traditions.

**Justification:** The CHI literature lacks historical depth. A paper that provides it will be heavily cited by everyone building on the CHI framework.

### Priority 6: Engage with Legal Scholarship on Overcriminalization
*Cross-references: Opportunity 7*

Acknowledge that statutory sentencing ranges emerge from a politically pathological process (Stuntz, Kleinfeld). Argue that core-offense ranges are insulated from the worst pathologies, and that transparent harm weighting enhances legitimacy (Haugh). This preempts an otherwise devastating critique of the CHI's weight source.

**Justification:** If a legal scholar reads a CHI paper and recognizes that the weight source is never interrogated, the project looks naive. Engaging proactively with Stuntz/Kleinfeld/Haugh turns a vulnerability into a strength and demonstrates interdisciplinary rigor.
