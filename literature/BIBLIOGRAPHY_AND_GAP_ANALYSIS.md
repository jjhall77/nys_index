## Part 3 — Conflicts and Debates (Quick-Reference Version)

### Debate 1: Sentencing Guidelines vs. Actual Sentences

| Camp | Key Citations | Core Claim |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Guidelines-based | Sherman et al. (2016); Sherman (2013); Lewis et al. (2024) | "Starting point" sentences are the purest victim-harm measure; actual sentences contaminated by prior records and racial disparities |
| Actual-sentences-based | House & Neyroud (2018); Karrholm et al. (2020); Curtis-Ham & Walton (2017); Wallace et al. (2009) | Most jurisdictions lack guidelines; actual sentences with filtering can approximate first-offender harm. Canada's CSI uses incarceration rate × average sentence. |
| Victim-perception-based | Ignatans & Pease (2016) | Victim-assessed seriousness (CSEW 1–20 scale) captures experiential harm that sentencing weights miss; challenges "democratic legitimacy" argument by centering victims |
| Philosophical/principled | Von Hirsch & Jareborg (1991); Greenfield & Paoli (2013) | Harm should be gauged by living-standard impact, not sentencing practice; ordinal not cardinal |
| Critical | Sarnecki (2021) | Average prison sentences inflate harm for crimes where prison is rare; legislative changes contaminate trends |

**Unresolved question:** Does the choice of sentencing metric materially change targeting decisions at the micro-place level?

**NYC angle:** Statutory midpoints from NYS Penal Law are a principled third way — democratic and transparent like guidelines, but available in any US jurisdiction with structured sentencing. The robustness analysis can test whether weight-range variation changes block targeting.

### Debate 2: Harm Spots vs. Hot Spots

| Camp | Key Citations | Core Claim |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Substantial divergence | Weinborn et al. (2017); Ratcliffe (2015); Fenimore (2019); Silva et al. (2025) | Harm spots reveal critical areas hot spots miss; Fenimore provides first US evidence (DC) with 40–65% spatial overlap |
| Context-dependent convergence | Ohyama et al. (2022) | In low-crime settings, minor crime volume dominates total harm |

**Unresolved question:** At what spatial resolution does the divergence emerge most clearly?

**NYC angle:** Block-level aggregation in a high-crime US city is the ideal test case.

### Debate 3: Police-Detected Crime Inclusion

| Camp | Key Citations | Core Claim |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Exclude | Sherman (2013); Sherman et al. (2016); Sherman & Associates (2020) | Policing outputs ≠ safety outcomes; the "fungibility fallacy" (Sherman 2013) |
| Include selectively | Andersen & Mueller-Johnson (2018); Ratcliffe (2015) | Targeting requires complete offender harm profiles |

**Unresolved question:** How to incorporate gun violence data that straddles victim-reported and police-detected categories?

**NYC angle:** The substitution approach resolves this for shootings by replacing (not adding to) complaint-based CHI.

### Debate 4: Equity and Feedback Loops

| Camp | Key Citations | Core Claim |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Feedback loop risk | Lewis et al. (2024) | Actual-sentencing indices embed racial disparities → deployment loop |
| Guidelines-based are safer | Lewis et al. (2024); Sherman et al. (2016) | Independent weights avoid the loop |

**Unresolved question:** Does statutory-range-based CHI (as used in the NYC project) perform equivalently to guidelines-based CHI in avoiding feedback loops?

**NYC angle:** The NYS Penal Law approach is structurally resistant to the Lewis et al. critique, since sentencing ranges are set by the legislature.

### Debate 5: Within-Category Granularity

| Camp | Key Citations | Core Claim |
|------|--------------|------------|
| Category-level sufficient | Sherman et al. (2016); Mitchell (2019) | Parsimony for trend analysis |
| Granularity matters | Curtis-Ham & Walton (2017); Ratcliffe & Kikuchi (2019) | Sub-category variation can exceed between-category variation |

**Unresolved question:** What is the optimal granularity level for block-level targeting?

**NYC angle:** PD-level weights (offense × police description) may be the most granular operational CHI in the US.

## Part 4 — Open Questions and Research Opportunities

### Opportunity 1: First US CHI Using Statutory Sentencing Midpoints
**Priority: HIGH**

**Gap:** No published US CHI uses the midpoint of structured sentencing ranges. Mitchell (2019) used maxima; Ratcliffe (2015) used gravity scores. Both are blunter instruments.

**My data:** NYPD complaint data (2018–2022) with ~500K+ records matched to NYS Penal Law codes; CHI lookup table mapping each law code to a sentencing-range midpoint in days.

**What I could do:** Publish the methodology and lookup table as a replicable "NYS Crime Harm Index" with validation against alternative weight specifications. The midpoint approach is generalizable to any US state with determinate sentencing — which is most of them. Frame as a methodological contribution that fills the gap between the Cambridge guidelines approach (unavailable in the US) and the California maximum approach (too blunt).

### Opportunity 2: Gun Violence Substitution Methodology
**Priority: HIGH**

**Gap:** No CHI paper addresses double-counting when shooting victims generate both complaint records and shooting incident records. No published work proposes a substitution methodology.

**My data:** NYPD shooting victim data (with fatal/non-fatal flag), NYPD complaint data, and a spatiotemporal matching algorithm (500 ft, 12 hours) for deduplication.

**What I could do:** Document the substitution approach as a general methodology for integrating incident-level and victim-level data in CHI pipelines. The key innovation: shooting CHI replaces (not adds to) arrest-based CHI for matched incidents, with fatal victims scored at Murder 2nd (7,300 days) and non-fatal at Attempted Murder 2nd (5,475 days). This resolves a structural problem that any jurisdiction using both complaint and shooting data will face.

### Opportunity 3: Block-Level Harm Concentration in a US City
**Priority: HIGH**

**Gap:** No published US study aggregates CHI to the block level. Existing work operates at the police-district level (Ratcliffe, 2015) or hot-spot level (Mitchell, 2019).

**My data:** NYC crime data geocoded to ~120K+ physical blocks; 5-year harm totals at block level; demographic and land-use overlays.

**What I could do:** Demonstrate the "power few" concentration of harm at the block level — the finest operational grain available. Compute Gini coefficients for harm vs. count concentration. Show how many blocks account for 50%/80%/90% of total CHI. Compare the top-N harm blocks vs. top-N count blocks and quantify overlap.

### Opportunity 4: Robustness of Micro-Place Targeting to CHI Specification
**Priority: HIGH**

**Gap:** No study tests whether the specific places selected for intervention change materially when CHI weights vary. Ashby (2018) compared methods at police-force level, not micro-place level.

**My data:** Block-level CHI computed under multiple specifications (low/mid/high weight ranges, with/without gun violence, borough-specific vs. citywide).

**What I could do:** Report overlap percentages and Spearman rank correlations across specifications for target lists of 200/300/400 blocks. If targeting is robust to CHI specification, this strengthens the operational case for any CHI. If not, this identifies which specification choices matter most — a methodological contribution.

### Opportunity 5: Scoring Shots-Fired Events
**Priority: MEDIUM**

**Gap:** No CHI paper addresses shots-fired incidents. These events represent real community harm but are absent from all existing indices.

**My data:** NYPD shots-fired complaint data classified by police description, with empirical distributions of charge severity.

**What I could do:** Propose a data-driven shots-fired scoring method using the distribution of associated criminal charges (with a CPW 2nd floor). Document as a replicable approach for jurisdictions with ShotSpotter or equivalent data.

### Opportunity 6: Temporal Stability of CHI Weights
**Priority: MEDIUM**

**Gap:** Most CHI papers report weights computed over a single period. No study tests year-over-year stability of CHI weights and its implications for long-term tracking.

**My data:** CHI weights computed separately for each year (2018–2022) at offense and PD levels.

**What I could do:** Report coefficients of variation and year-over-year changes for each weight. Identify which offense categories have stable vs. volatile weights. This matters for whether CHI-based targeting can use fixed weights or needs annual recalibration.

### Opportunity 7: Equity Audit of Statutory-Midpoint CHI
**Priority: MEDIUM**

**Gap:** Lewis et al. (2024) identified feedback loop risks for actual-sentencing indices. No equivalent analysis exists for statutory-range indices in the US context.

**My data:** Block-level CHI with demographic overlays; statutory-midpoint weights that are structurally independent of judicial decisions.

**What I could do:** Explicitly test whether the NYS statutory-midpoint approach avoids the Lewis et al. feedback loop concern by comparing demographic profiles of targeted blocks across specifications. Frame as an equity validation of the chosen methodology.

## Part 5 — Recommended Research Priorities

### Priority 1: The NYS Crime Harm Index — Methodology and Validation
*Cross-references: Opportunities 1, 4, 6*

Publish the statutory-midpoint CHI methodology as a replicable framework for US jurisdictions. Include the lookup table, validation against alternative specifications, and temporal stability analysis. This fills the largest gap in the US CHI literature and provides the foundation for all other contributions.

**Justification:** No US CHI uses statutory midpoints. The approach is generalizable to all states with determinate sentencing. It avoids the Lewis et al. (2024) equity critique. The robustness analysis provides immediate practical guidance for operational adoption.

### Priority 2: Gun Violence and the CHI — A Substitution Approach
*Cross-references: Opportunities 2, 5*

Document the substitution methodology for integrating shooting data into CHI pipelines without double-counting. Include the shots-fired scoring method. This addresses a structural problem that no existing CHI paper has confronted.

**Justification:** Gun violence is the most policy-relevant crime category in US cities. Any US CHI that does not address shootings is incomplete. The substitution approach is methodologically novel and practically essential.

### Priority 3: Block-Level Harm Concentration and Targeting
*Cross-references: Opportunities 3, 4*

Demonstrate the power-few concentration of harm at the block level in NYC, with robustness testing across specifications. This is the operational payoff of the index — the evidence that specific blocks can be reliably identified for place-based intervention.

**Justification:** Place-based intervention requires knowing *where* to intervene. This analysis shows whether CHI-based targeting is reliable enough to act on, and how sensitive targeting is to methodological choices.

### Priority 4: Equity Validation
*Cross-references: Opportunity 7*

Conduct an equity audit of the statutory-midpoint CHI, testing whether targeted blocks change across demographic strata under different specifications. Explicitly engage with Lewis et al. (2024).

**Justification:** Any harm-weighted policing tool must address equity concerns. Proactively conducting this analysis strengthens the case for adoption and preempts criticism. The statutory-midpoint approach is structurally positioned to perform well on this test.

### Priority 5: CHI-Based Performance Metrics for NYC Precincts
*Cross-references: Synthesis Section 1 (Cambridge Consensus)*

Compute the Harm Detection Fraction (HDF) proposed by Sherman & Associates (2020) at the precinct level for NYC: what proportion of block-level CHI has an associated arrest or clearance? This operationalizes the Cambridge Consensus framework in a major US city for the first time.

**Justification:** Directly implements the most ambitious recommendation of the Cambridge Consensus. Provides a new accountability metric that moves beyond crime counts.
